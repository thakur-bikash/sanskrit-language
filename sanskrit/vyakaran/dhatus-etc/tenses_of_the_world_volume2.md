## Preface: The Remainder

The first volume ended with a confession: Dravidian languages, Austronesian focus-marking, Algonquian animate grammar, Tibetan evidentials, and others had been left out not for lack of interest but for lack of space. This volume gives them the depth they deserve.

But first — a thought carried forward from the first volume.

Every grammatical system is a *forced choice*. When a language makes you mark evidentiality on every verb, it means that at some point in the history of that community, the question *how do you know this?* became important enough to grammaticalize — to make mandatory, reflexive, automatic, sub-conscious. You can no longer avoid answering it. It is baked into your tongue.

Similarly, when Tamil forces you to mark whether the subject of a verb is *rational* or *non-rational*, it means that the distinction between beings who have deliberate will and those who do not has been made grammatically inescapable. You cannot describe a dog's action and a person's action with the same verb form without implying something about the moral and ontological status of each.

And when Tagalog builds its entire grammar around *focus* — around which participant in the event is being given semantic prominence — it tells you that the Tagalog-speaking world is one where the *choice of perspective* is not a stylistic flourish but a grammatical fact about a sentence.

These are not curiosities. They are philosophies expressed in grammar.

## PART ONE: THE DRAVIDIAN TRADITION

---

### Tamil: One of the World's Oldest Living Literary Languages

Tamil has continuous literary documentation going back over two thousand years — the *Sangam* poetry (roughly 300 BCE – 300 CE) is among the oldest secular literature on earth. Its grammar was codified in the *Tolkāppiyam* (perhaps the world's oldest surviving grammatical treatise still in use as a reference) and elaborated in the medieval *Nannūl*. Tamil is spoken by over 80 million people across Tamil Nadu, Sri Lanka, Singapore, Malaysia, and the Tamil diaspora worldwide.

What makes Tamil grammatically extraordinary for our purposes:

1. A deeply developed aspect system with multiple past tenses.
2. The **Rational / Non-rational** (uyar tiṇai / aḵṟiṇai) distinction built into the verb system.
3. A separate *written* and *spoken* grammatical tradition — the diglossic split between Classical Tamil (*Centamiḻ*) and Colloquial Tamil (*Koṭuntamiḻ*) — where the two varieties have *different grammatical rules for the same sentences*.
4. A set of aspect auxiliaries (similar to Japanese *-te iru*, *-te oku*, *-te shimau*) that encode not just what an action's completion status is but its *social and emotional character*.

---

### The Three Tenses of Tamil

Tamil has three tenses marked by infixes inserted between the verb root and the personal ending:

---

#### **இறந்த காலம்** *Iṟanta Kālam* — The Past That Has Passed Away

The word *iṟanta* means "that which has passed, that which has died." The past is *literally* the tense of what has died — what has passed out of the living present.

The tense marker varies by verb class — Tamil verbs are divided into classes (kuṟippu vakai) based on the nature of their stem, and the past-tense infix differs:

- Verbs ending in -gu: past marker **-n-**
- Strong verbs: past marker **-tt-** (a geminated, doubled consonant — the linguistic "weight" of completion)
- Weak verbs: past marker **-nt-**

> **வந்தான்** *vantāṉ* — He came (√va- + past marker -nt- + 3rd person masculine ending -āṉ).
> **படித்தாள்** *paṭittāḷ* — She studied (√paṭi- + past marker -tt- + 3rd person feminine ending -āḷ).

What is grammatically compulsory here, that English does not require, is the **person-gender-number ending** that terminates every Tamil finite verb. You cannot say "came" without also saying *who* came and what *gender* and *rational/non-rational status* they have. The verb form is a complete grammatical sentence in miniature.

---

#### **நிகழ் காலம்** *Nikaḻ Kālam* — The Present That Is Happening

*Nikaḻ* means "that which is occurring, unfolding, happening." The present tense infix **-kiṟ-** (in formal Tamil) or **-r-** or **-kkiṟ-** (in various colloquial forms) marks the present:

> **படிக்கிறான்** *paṭikkiṟāṉ* — He is studying / he studies.

Critically: Tamil's present tense form covers both *right now* and *habitually*. The same form means "he is studying (at this moment)" and "he studies (as his habit or occupation)." Context and adverbs distinguish them. This is aspect-neutral — Tamil's present is simply *the non-past, non-future*.

---

#### **எதிர் காலம்** *Etir Kālam* — The Future That Faces You

*Etir* means "facing, opposite, coming toward you." The future is what stands *opposite* — what faces you as it approaches. The future tense infix is **-v-** (or **-pp-** in certain verb classes):

> **படிப்பான்** *paṭippāṉ* — He will study.

---

### The Rational / Non-Rational Distinction — உயர்திணை / அஃறிணை

This is the feature that most distinguishes Tamil's morphology from Indo-European languages. Every Tamil noun, pronoun, and finite verb belongs to one of two **tiṇai** (classes):

**உயர்திணை** *Uyartiṇai* — "the exalted class" — humans and divine beings (gods, deified saints, sometimes the sun and moon by virtue of divine association).

**அஃறிணை** *Aḵṟiṇai* — "the low class" — animals, objects, abstract concepts, and everything else.

This distinction runs through the *entire* verb ending system. The third-person endings of every Tamil verb are different depending on whether the subject is rational-human or non-rational:

| Person | Rational (Human/Divine) | Non-Rational (Animal/Object) |
|---|---|---|
| 3rd sing. masculine | -āṉ (he) | -atu (it) |
| 3rd sing. feminine | -āḷ (she) | -atu (it) |
| 3rd plural | -ārkaḷ (they, human) | -aṉa (they, non-human) |

> **அவன் வந்தான்** *avaṉ vantāṉ* — He (male human) came.
> **நாய் வந்தது** *nāy vantatu* — The dog came (non-rational ending).
> **மரம் விழுந்தது** *maram viḻuntatu* — The tree fell (non-rational ending).

What this means philosophically: *agency* and *will* are built into Tamil grammar. When you use the rational ending, you are implicitly attributing *deliberate, intentional action* to the subject. When you use the non-rational ending, you are marking the event as something that *happened to or through* a being without full rational will.

This creates an interesting moral complication when describing animal behavior. A Tamil speaker who uses the rational ending for an animal is *elevating* that animal — perhaps out of affection, or to attribute surprising intelligence or will. Pet owners often do this. Conversely, using a non-rational ending for a human in a story can be a profound insult — it strips them of the grammatical markers of rational will and places them among things that simply happen.

---

### The Tamil Aspect Auxiliary System — A Grammar of Emotional Completion

Tamil has a set of *aspect auxiliaries* — helper verbs that follow the main verb (in its *verbal participle* form, the **-u** or **-a** form) to express the *character* of the action's completion. These are similar to Japanese *-te shimau*, *-te oku*, *-te miru* — but Tamil's system is remarkably rich.

The main verb appears as a *verbal participle* (a non-finite form ending in **-u** or **-a**) and the auxiliary carries the full conjugation for tense, person, and number. Together they form a *compound verb* that expresses not just what happened but how the speaker *feels* about how it happened, or what the action's relationship to the world is.

---

#### **விடு** *Viṭu* — "To Release / Let Go" → The Completive with a Sense of Finality or Relief

*Viṭu* (to release, to let go, to leave) as an auxiliary marks *complete, total, irreversible* action — action that has been thoroughly done and is now *let go*, *released into the past*. It can carry a sense of: the thing is fully done, it has been released, it is gone.

Depending on context, this ranges from:
- Neutral completion: just emphasizing that the action is thoroughly done.
- Relief: *thank goodness it's done*.
- Regret: *it's done now and can't be undone* (similar to Japanese *-te shimau*).
- Dismissal: *it's gone, it's over, don't worry about it*.

> **சாப்பிட்டுவிட்டான்** *cāppiṭṭuviṭṭāṉ* — He has eaten (and it's done / he finished eating / the food is gone).
> **கொன்றுவிட்டான்** *koṉṟuviṭṭāṉ* — He killed (him) — (it's done, irreversibly, the deed is released into the past — often with a heavy finality).
> **போய்விட்டான்** *pōyviṭṭāṉ* — He has gone (and is now away, that departure is complete and irreversible).

The *viṭu* auxiliary teaches you something: in Tamil thought, completion and release are the same motion. To *finish* doing something is to *let it go*.

---

#### **கொள்** *Koḷ* — "To Take / Hold for Oneself" → The Reflexive Benefactive Completive

*Koḷ* (to take, to hold, to keep) marks that the action is done *for the subject's own benefit, use, or sake*. The subject acts and the result accrues to *themselves*:

> **படித்துக்கொண்டான்** *paṭittukkoṇṭāṉ* — He studied (for himself, for his own benefit, on his own account).
> **சாப்பிட்டுக்கொண்டான்** *cāppiṭṭukkoṇṭāṉ* — He ate (for his own sustenance — no one else was involved or benefited).

This is distinct from the simple past because the simple past is neutral about who benefits. The *koḷ* compound makes explicit: the subject did this *for themselves*. It can also carry a tone of *self-sufficiency* ("he managed it himself"), sometimes mildly self-absorbed ("he went ahead and did it for himself without involving others").

In requests and commands, *koḷ* marks polite self-service:
> **உட்கார்ந்துக்கோ** *uṭkārntukō* — Please sit down (for yourself — be comfortable, take care of yourself).

---

#### **வை** *Vai* — "To Place / Keep" → The Preparatory, the Done-Ahead-of-Time

*Vai* (to place, to put, to keep) marks that an action is done *in advance, in preparation, or stored for future use*. It is exactly parallel to Japanese *-te oku* ("to do something and put it away for later").

> **சமைத்துவைத்தாள்** *camaittuvaiттāḷ* — She cooked (in advance, in preparation for a meal to come / she made food and kept it ready).
> **எழுதிவைத்திருக்கிறேன்** *eḻutivaittirukkiṟēṉ* — I have written it (down, and stored it — it's there, ready, preserved).

The action has been *placed in readiness*. Things done with *vai* are done with a forward-looking orientation — they are actions that create future availability. A mother who *sāppiṭṭu vai* cooks stores the meal for her family; the action is not complete for its own sake but as *preparation for being used*.

---

#### **பார்** *Pār* — "To See / Look" → The Experiential / Tentative

*Pār* (to see, to look) as an auxiliary marks *trying something to see the result* — exactly parallel to Japanese *-te miru* ("to do and see"). It is the grammar of *experiment, attempt, and openness to outcome*:

> **சாப்பிட்டுப்பார்** *cāppiṭṭupār* — Try eating it / Eat it and see (how it is).
> **கேட்டுப்பாரேன்** *kēṭṭupārēṉ* — I'll ask (and see what happens).

The *pār* auxiliary implies: *I am performing this action, and I am holding myself open to what I find*. It is the opposite of doing something with certainty about the outcome. When you tell someone to *cāppiṭṭupār* ("eat and see"), you are inviting them to be curious about the experience.

---

#### **போ** *Pō* — "To Go" → The Completed-Away, the Gone and Done With

*Pō* (to go) as an auxiliary marks completion where the result is *departure, permanent change of state, or the passing away of something*:

> **மறந்துபோனான்** *maṟantupōṉāṉ* — He forgot (it went away from him — his memory departed).
> **இறந்துபோனான்** *iṟantupōṉāṉ* — He died (he went away into death).
> **உடைந்துபோச்சு** *uṭaintupōccu* — It broke (and is now gone from its former state — colloquial form).

The *pō* auxiliary has a tone of *irreversibility and loss* — the action has *gone away* and taken something with it. It is used for deaths, breakages, disappearances, forgettings, and the departure of conditions that once held.

---

#### **கொடு** *Koṭu* — "To Give" → The Benefactive Outward (Doing Something For Another)

*Koṭu* (to give) marks that the action is performed *for someone else's benefit*:

> **சாப்பிட்டுக்கொடுத்தாள்** *cāppiṭṭukkoṭuttāḷ* — She fed him (gave the eating to him — served him, cooked and gave him food).
> **எழுதிக்கொடு** *eḻutikkoṭu* — Write it (for me / for them — as a gift of your labor).

Compare with *koḷ*: where *koḷ* means "do this for yourself," *koṭu* means "do this as a gift to another." Together they form a benefactive pair that encodes the *direction of the action's benefit* — inward toward self, or outward toward another.

This matters enormously in social language. Asking someone to write something with *eḻutu* (plain imperative) is a command. Asking with *eḻutikkoṭu* is: please write this *as a gift to me*. The *koṭu* form makes the request a kind of *receiving*, framing the other person's action as a giving. It is more polite precisely because it acknowledges your own dependence on their generosity.

---

#### **வா** *Vā* — "To Come" → Action Moving Toward the Speaker or Toward Us

*Vā* (to come) as an auxiliary marks motion or action that *comes toward the speaker's sphere*, or action that is *progressively developing* toward the speaker:

> **வளர்ந்துவருகிறது** *vaḷarntu varukiratu* — It is growing (coming along, developing toward us).
> **படிக்கிறான் வருகிறான்** — He is gradually coming to study more (idiom of progressive development).

The *vā* auxiliary traces a *trajectory toward* — things that are becoming, things that are moving into the speaker's present.

---

#### The Interplay: Stacking Aspect Auxiliaries

Tamil speakers sometimes stack these auxiliaries or choose among them based on *emotional stance* rather than any logical necessity. The same event can be described with different auxiliaries to shift the emotional frame:

A broken vase:
- **உடைந்துபோச்சு** (*pō* auxiliary) — It broke (and now that state of wholeness is gone — loss, irreversibility).
- **உடைத்துவிட்டான்** (*viṭu* auxiliary) — He broke it (and the deed is done, released — perhaps with blame: he went and broke it).

A meal eaten:
- **சாப்பிட்டுவிட்டான்** (*viṭu*) — He ate (it's done and over — the food is gone, he finished, possibly it was consumed that he shouldn't have).
- **சாப்பிட்டுக்கொண்டான்** (*koḷ*) — He ate (for himself — self-sufficiently, on his own, perhaps privately or without offering to share).

These are not neutral alternatives. Choosing one over the other is a statement about the speaker's stance toward the event.

---

### Classical Tamil's *Kuṟiñci* and the Grammar of Place-Feeling

The ancient Tamil *akam* (interior) poetry tradition organized human emotional experience through a system called *tiṇai* — *landscapes of feeling*. Each landscape (*kuṟiñci* for the mountains, *mullai* for the forest, *marutam* for the farmland, *neytal* for the seashore, *pālai* for the desert) was associated with a particular emotional situation, a particular season, a particular bird, flower, time of day, and deity. When a classical Tamil poet described a kuṟiñci bird call at dusk, every reader knew this evoked: union, the joy of lovers meeting secretly in the mountains at night.

This is not a grammatical form. But it is a *semantics of time* that shapes how Tamil speakers conceive of events. The *tiṇai* tradition encodes: *what kind of time is this?* not by verb tense but by the landscape you invoke. Evening and the seashore is *separation and longing*. Forest and autumn is *patient waiting*. The grammar of Tamil time is not only in its verb system — it is in its entire poetic cosmology.

## PART TWO: THE AUSTRONESIAN FAMILY

---

### Tagalog: Where Perspective Is Grammatically Mandatory

Tagalog (and its standardized form Filipino) is spoken by 25–30 million people as a first language and by nearly 90 million Filipinos as a national language. It belongs to the Philippine branch of the Austronesian family and shares its core grammatical structure with hundreds of related Philippine languages (Cebuano, Ilocano, Hiligaynon, Kapampangan, and others).

Tagalog's grammar is built around a feature that has no real equivalent in any of the world's major literary languages of Europe, China, or the Middle East: the **focus system** (also called the *trigger system*, or in older terminology the *voice system*). Understanding this system is the key to understanding Philippine grammar and to asking the deeper question of what grammar reveals about how speakers perceive events.

---

### The Fundamental Problem: What Is This Sentence About?

In English, word order tells you the subject: "The man hit the ball" — the man did the hitting. "The ball was hit by the man" — the ball is now the subject, the man is demoted to agent.

Tagalog does something fundamentally different. In Tagalog, a special marker on the *verb* — called the **focus marker** or **trigger marker** — announces: *among all the participants in this event, this particular one is the thing I am making this sentence about*. And that announcement changes the form of the verb.

The *ang*-marked noun in Tagalog is the **topic** or **focus** — the participant being spotlighted, the one the sentence is fundamentally about. The verb's focus marker tells you *what kind of participant* is being spotlighted.

The same event — a man buying rice at a market — can be expressed in multiple ways in Tagalog, each grammatically correct, each foregrounding a *different participant*:

---

#### **Actor Focus (AF)** — The Spotlight Is on the One Who Does the Action

The verb takes the actor-focus infix **-um-** (or prefix **mag-**):

> **Bumili ang lalaki ng bigas sa tindahan** — *The man* bought rice at the store.

*Ang lalaki* is marked with *ang* — the focus/topic marker. The verb *bumili* (from *bili*, "buy") carries **-um-** to show: the spotlighted participant is the one *doing the buying*. The rice (*bigas*) and the store (*tindahan*) are present in the sentence but marked with *ng* and *sa* — they are participants, but not the topic.

This sentence is fundamentally *about the man*. His being an actor, his buying, is the point.

---

#### **Object Focus (OF)** — The Spotlight Is on What Was Bought

The verb takes the object-focus suffix **-in** (or **-in** variants):

> **Binili ng lalaki ang bigas sa tindahan** — *The rice* was bought by the man at the store.

Now *ang bigas* (the rice) is focus-marked. The verb *binili* carries **-in** to say: the thing being spotlighted is the *thing being bought*. The man (*lalaki*) is still present but now marked with *ng* — he is the agent but not the topic.

This is *not* a passive construction in the English sense. The man is still the agent, still the active buyer. We have not removed his agency or demoted him — we have simply shifted which participant the sentence is *about*. The rice is now the grammatical center of gravity.

---

#### **Locative Focus (LF)** — The Spotlight Is on Where

The verb takes the locative-focus suffix **-an**:

> **Binilhan ng lalaki ng bigas ang tindahan** — *The store* is where the man bought rice.

*Ang tindahan* (the store) is now spotlighted. The verb *binilhan* carries **-an** to announce: the thing being topicalized is the *location* of the buying. The store is not the store "at which" buying happened as an afterthought — it is the *subject* of the sentence's concern.

---

#### **Benefactive Focus (BF)** — The Spotlight Is on Whom the Action Was Done For

The verb takes the benefactive-focus suffix **-an** (distinct from locative by context and often by the presence of *para sa*):

> **Ibinili ng lalaki ng bigas ang babae** — *The woman* was the one for whom the man bought rice.

The woman (*ang babae*) is now topicalized. She didn't buy anything; the man bought for her. The verb *ibinili* (with prefix *i-*) marks: the spotlighted participant is the *recipient/beneficiary* of the buying.

---

### What the Focus System Reveals

Consider what this grammar is doing: it is making *perspective* grammatically mandatory.

In English, you can speak about an event without consciously choosing which participant you are treating as the subject of your attention. The default — the agent — is almost automatic. Passive constructions allow you to shift focus, but they are *marked* forms — they feel deliberately chosen, often passive-voiced and somewhat awkward.

In Tagalog, there is no "default" perspective. You *must* choose a focus. The language requires you, for every event, to decide: *which aspect of this event am I making the center of this sentence?* The man? The thing he got? The place it happened? The person he did it for?

This is not just about grammar. It is about *what you are paying attention to*. In a Tagalog-speaking community, the choice of focus can communicate subtle information about what matters in a situation. If someone asks "Why is the store out of rice?" you would answer with a store-focus sentence — the store is your concern. If someone asks "What did he buy?" you would use an object-focus sentence — the rice is your concern. If someone asks "Where did he get it?" the location-focus sentence is the natural choice.

---

### The Aspect System in Tagalog: Completed vs. Contemplated

Tagalog does not have tense in the way that European languages have tense. It has **aspect** — specifically the distinction between:

- **Completed** (*naganap na*): the action is done.
- **Incompleted** (*hindi pa natapos*): the action is not yet done — which covers both "currently happening" and "not yet begun but intended or expected."

These two aspects combine with the focus system to create a matrix. Each combination is a different verb form:

| | Actor Focus | Object Focus | Locative Focus | Benefactive Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Completed** | b**um**ili | b**in**ili | bin**il**han | ib**in**ili |
| **Incompleted** | b**um**ibili | bilib**il**in | bib**il**han | ib**il**i |
| **Contemplated/Infinitive** | b**um**ili | bilib**in** | bil**han** | ib**il**i |

The "contemplated" form (sometimes called the *infinitive* or *future*) is used:
- After modal verbs ("I want to buy," "I need to buy," "I will buy")
- For commands
- In certain dependent clauses

There is no morphological way to say *when* something happened — only *whether it is done or not*.

> **Bumili ka ng bigas** — Go buy rice / Buy some rice (command, contemplated aspect, actor focus).
> **Bumibili ka ng bigas** — You are buying rice / You buy rice (incompleted, ongoing or habitual).
> **Bumili ka ng bigas** (completed aspect with completed inflection) — You bought rice.

Time — yesterday vs. tomorrow — is established through time words (*kahapon*, yesterday; *bukas*, tomorrow; *kanina*, just now) not through verb forms. Tagalog grammar insists on knowing whether something is *done or not done* — not when it was done.

---

### The *Ligature* and the Grammar of Connection

Tagalog uses a particle called the **ligature** — **na** (after consonants) or **-ng** (after vowels) — to link modifiers to what they modify. This ligature is one of the most frequent words in Tagalog and signals: *these two things are in a modification relationship*.

> **Magandang babae** — Beautiful woman (*maganda* + *-ng* + *babae*).
> **Lalaking matalino** — Man (who is) intelligent (*lalaki* + *-ng* + *matalino*).

The ligature does not distinguish adjective from relative clause — both are simply linked. A *magandang babae* is "a woman linked-to-beautiful" — which means "a beautiful woman." A *lalaking bumibili ng bigas* is "a man linked-to-buying-rice" — which means "a man who is buying rice" or "the rice-buying man."

This means Tagalog relative clauses are built by the same linking mechanism as adjective phrases — the grammar does not distinguish between a permanent quality ("a beautiful woman") and a temporary action ("a man who is buying rice"). Both are *modifications linked by na/-ng*. The distinction between essence and circumstance that Irish encodes in its two copulas (*is* vs. *tá*) is here dissolved into a single, simple ligature.

---

### Cebuano, Ilocano, and the Philippine Spread

The focus system described above is not unique to Tagalog — it runs through the entire Philippine branch of Austronesian. Cebuano (spoken by roughly 25 million in the Visayas and Mindanao) has a nearly identical system. Ilocano (the language of northern Luzon) has its own elaboration of focus. The Proto-Philippine language from which all these descended had focus-marking as a core grammatical feature, and it has been preserved and elaborated in different ways across dozens of languages.

What is extraordinary is that this system — which requires speakers to grammatically mark *perspective and topicality* on every transitive verb — has been stable for thousands of years across a geographically scattered island chain. It suggests that the cognitive habit of *choosing what a sentence is about* is very deeply ingrained in Philippine linguistic culture.

---

### Malay / Indonesian: A Closely Related Language Without Philippine-Style Focus

For comparison: Malay and Indonesian, which are also Austronesian but belong to a different (western Malayo-Polynesian) branch, have a much simpler system. Their verb forms distinguish *active* (prefix **me-**) and *passive* (prefix **di-**), but without the full focus array of Tagalog:

> **Saya membeli beras** — I bought rice (active, me- prefix, subject is actor).
> **Beras dibeli (oleh) saya** — Rice was bought by me (passive, di- prefix, patient becomes subject).

This is more like the English active/passive distinction. The Philippine focus system, by contrast, is far more articulated — it is not just about active vs. passive but about *which of four possible participants is topicalized*, with different verb forms for each.

## PART THREE: THE ALGONQUIAN FAMILY

---

### Algonquian: The Animacy Grammar of the Eastern Woodlands

The Algonquian language family is one of the most geographically widespread in North America, stretching from the Atlantic coast deep into the Great Plains. It includes Ojibwe/Anishinaabe, Cree (in many dialects), Algonquin, Menominee, Fox/Mesquakie, Potawatomi, Mi'kmaq, Delaware/Lenape, Shawnee, Arapaho, Blackfoot, and over thirty others. Most of these languages share a core grammatical architecture that is radically different from both European and East Asian languages.

The single most important feature of this architecture: the **animate / inanimate** distinction, which runs through nouns, verbs, pronouns, and most grammatical categories, and which does not map onto biological life in any simple way.

---

### Animate vs. Inanimate: Not What You Think

In most European languages, grammatical gender (masculine/feminine/neuter) is an arbitrary classification — swords are masculine in German, moons are feminine in French, neither has anything to do with actual gender. Algonquian animacy *also* does not map neatly onto biological life, but it carries *cultural weight* that grammatical gender in European languages usually lacks.

In Ojibwe (Anishinaabemowin):

**Animate nouns** include: all humans, all animals — but also: **raspberries, strawberries** (other berries are inanimate), **kettles, drums, canoes, snowshoes, pipes, nets, certain medicines, certain stones, the sun, the moon, thunder**. Trees are animate; fallen logs can be inanimate. Snowshoes (the tools that allow movement across winter land) are animate; shoes generally are not.

**Inanimate nouns** include: rocks generally (though certain special stones are animate), most tools, most objects, lakes and rivers (though animate water spirits are invoked in some ceremonial contexts), words on a page.

The animacy of an object in Algonquian grammar reflects *its status in Anishinaabe relationships with the world* — things that are animate are things that participate in the web of relationships that constitute being alive and being in community. A drum is animate because it has a voice, a spirit, a relationship to ceremony. A kettle is animate because it is intimately associated with the preparation of food, with sustenance, with gathering. A canoe is animate because it moves, because it is the vehicle of relationship between human beings and water.

This is not a naïve projection of life onto objects. Anishinaabe speakers do not literally believe a kettle breathes. But they do recognize that some objects participate more fully in the web of relationships that constitute meaningful life, and grammar marks that recognition as obligatory.

---

### The Verb at the Center: Everything Is Verbal

Algonquian languages are famously *verb-heavy*. In Ojibwe, it is estimated that over 80% of the vocabulary consists of verb forms — vastly more than in most of the world's languages. Things that English describes with nouns and adjectives, Ojibwe often describes with verbs:

> **Miskwaa** — It is red (not "it is red" with a separate adjective, but a verb meaning "to be in a state of redness").
> **Anishinaabe** — A person (literally: "one who is in right relation, one who is as one should be" — a verbal concept).
> **Ishkode** — Fire (literally: "there is fire here" — a locative-existential verb form).

This means that describing the world in Ojibwe involves primarily *predicating states and actions*, not naming objects and assigning properties to them. The world is described as a *field of events and states* rather than as a *collection of objects with properties*.

---

### The Four-Way Verb Classification in Algonquian

All Algonquian verbs are classified by *two binary features*:

1. **Whether the verb is animate or inanimate** in its grammatical class.
2. **Whether the verb is intransitive or transitive**.

This creates four verb classes:

| | Intransitive (no object) | Transitive (has an object) |
|---|---|---|
| **Animate** | VAI — Verb Animate Intransitive | VTA — Verb Transitive Animate |
| **Inanimate** | VII — Verb Inanimate Intransitive | VTI — Verb Transitive Inanimate |

**VAI** verbs: have animate subjects, no objects.
> *Bimaadizi* — He/she/it(anim) lives, is alive, moves through life.
> *Bimibatoo* — He/she runs.

**VII** verbs: have inanimate subjects.
> *Minaagozi* — It(inanim) is beautiful.
> *Gizhide* — It is hot.

**VTA** verbs: have animate subjects and animate objects — beings acting on beings.
> *Waabamaad* — He/she sees him/her/it(anim).

**VTI** verbs: have animate subjects and inanimate objects — beings acting on things.
> *Waabandaan* — He/she sees it(inanim).

The word "see" is *two different words in Ojibwe* depending on whether you are seeing a person/animate being (**VTA**) or seeing a thing (**VTI**). To use the wrong form would imply something false about the nature of what is being seen.

---

### The Proximate / Obviative Distinction — Third-Person Grammar for Two "Thirds"

One of the most distinctive features of Algonquian grammar is the **proximate / obviative** distinction, which handles the problem that many sentences have *two* third-person participants (he/she and he/she/it again) — and you need to keep track of which is which.

In English, this is done through context and sometimes becomes genuinely ambiguous: "John told James that he had failed" — *he* could be John or James. You have to figure it out from context.

Algonquian languages make this grammatically unambiguous through two "levels" of third person:

**Proximate** (*the primary third person, the one in focus*): marked with a plain third-person form. This is the participant currently "on stage," the one the story or sentence is primarily about.

**Obviative** (*the secondary third person, the one offstage or less prominent*): marked with a special obviative suffix (often **-an** or **-ini** in Ojibwe). This is a second third person — another person or being in the sentence who is not currently the primary concern.

> **Ogimaa waabamaad odanissan** — The chief (prox) sees his daughter (obv).

The chief is proximate — he is the primary topic. The daughter is obviative — she is also present but secondary. If the sentence shifted to be about the daughter's perspective:

> **Odanis ogimaanh waabamaad** — The daughter (prox) sees the chief (obv).

Now the daughter is proximate and the chief, the same chief who was just proximate, is marked obviative.

This creates a narrative perspective marker built into the grammar: **who is proximate at any given moment in a story is essentially the character being followed, the one through whose perspective the narration flows.** When a story shifts focus from one character to another, it is marked by the shift between proximate and obviative. Listeners track this as automatically as readers of a novel track point-of-view.

---

### The Inverse System: When the Object Outranks the Subject

Algonquian languages have a remarkable feature called the **inverse** (or *inverse voice*): the direction of action between proximate and obviative participants is marked in the verb by either a **direct** or **inverse** form.

**Direct**: when a proximate participant acts on an obviative one. This is the "expected" direction — the one in focus acts on the one not in focus.

**Inverse**: when an obviative participant acts on a proximate one. This is the "unexpected" or "reversed" direction.

In English, "he (prox) saw her (obv)" and "she (obv) saw him (prox)" are completely different sentences with the agent and patient swapped. In Algonquian, they are the *same verb form* — but with either a direct or inverse marker:

> **Waabamaad** (direct): He (prox) sees her (obv). The expected direction — the proximate acts.
> **Waaabamigod** (inverse): He (prox) is seen by her (obv). The unexpected direction — the obviative acts on the proximate.

The inverse form is not a passive — the agent (the seeing entity) is still fully present and grammatically real. But it is a marker that the action is flowing "against" the expected proximate-acts-on-obviative direction.

This is a profound difference from the European active/passive distinction. In English passive ("he was seen by her"), the agent is demoted, made optional, backgrounded. In Algonquian inverse, *both participants are fully present and marked* — it is simply the direction of action that is being noted. The grammar is tracking *who acts on whom*, and marking when the flow goes the unexpected way.

---

### The Algonquian Verb as a Complete Sentence

Algonquian verbs are *polysynthetic* — they incorporate enormous amounts of information that European languages would spread across multiple words:

In Cree, the word **niwāpahtamawāw** is a single verb form that means: "I see it (inanimate) for him (animate)." Broken down:

- **ni-** : I (1st person subject)
- **-wāpaht-** : see (verb root, VTI)
- **-amaw-** : for someone (benefactive/applicative extension — I'm seeing it *on behalf of* a third party)
- **-āw** : third-person animate singular object (marking the "him" for whom the seeing is done)

This one word carries: agent, action, beneficiary, and both the direct object (inanimate thing seen) and the indirect object (animate person for whom one sees) — all in a single morphological unit.

In Menominee, verb forms can be even longer, incorporating instrumental information (with what tool? by what means?), locative information (where exactly?), and causative information (causing what?).

---

### Ojibwe Tense and the Non-Tense System

Ojibwe has a grammatical marker often called a "tense particle" but which is really closer to a *temporal discourse particle* — it operates at the clause level, not inflected into the verb:

**gii-** : past tense marker, prefixed to the verb.
**ga-** (or **da-**): future/expected marker.
**wii-** : intentional/desire marker — something the subject *wants* or *intends* to do.

> **Gii-miijiw** — He/she ate (past: gii- prefix on eat).
> **Ga-miijiw** — He/she will eat (future: ga- prefix).
> **Wii-miijiw** — He/she wants to eat / is going to eat (intentional).

But these prefixes are not obligatory in the way that tense morphology is in, say, Latin or Turkish. Ojibwe speakers can and do use bare verb forms without temporal marking, relying on context. The aspectual-evidential information in the verb (VAI/VTI/VTA/VII, proximate/obviative, direct/inverse) is *more* grammatically fundamental than tense.

## PART FOUR: THE TIBETO-BURMAN TRADITION

---

### Tibetan: The Language That Grammaticalizes Intention, Will, and Witness

Classical and Modern Tibetan (both Lhasa colloquial and the classical *Chöke* literary standard) have a verb system that is, in some respects, the most complex evidential and egophorical system in the world. It is extraordinarily demanding for learners — not because it is arbitrary, but because it requires the speaker to *situate themselves in relation to the action* in ways that English simply does not require.

The key concepts:

1. **Evidentiality**: as in Turkish and Quechua, Tibetan marks how the speaker knows what they claim to know.
2. **Egophoricity**: a feature found in Tibetan and several Himalayan languages — verbs are marked differently depending on whether the subject is the *speaker themselves* or someone else. First-person volitional action and third-person volitional action are grammatically distinct.
3. **Volitionality**: whether the subject *intended* or *controlled* the action affects which verb form is used.
4. **Multiple copulas**: Tibetan has several verbs corresponding to English "be" and "have," each encoding a different relationship between the speaker and the state being described.

---

### The Evidential Copulas of Tibetan

Tibetan has *multiple copulas* (multiple "to be" / "to exist" forms) where each one encodes the *speaker's relationship to the state being described*. This is not about tense or aspect — it is about *epistemological stance*:

---

#### **ཡིན** *yin* — First-Person Assertive Copula

Used when the speaker is asserting something about *themselves*, or asserting a definitional/classificatory fact that the speaker *knows with full certainty from within*:

> **ང་བོད་པ་ཡིན** *ngā Böpa yin* — I am Tibetan (this is a fact about myself; I know it from the inside; I am asserting my own identity).

*Yin* is the copula of *first-person certainty* and *definitional classification*. When you use *yin* for something about yourself, you are claiming the deepest possible knowledge — the knowledge of your own nature.

---

#### **རེད** *red* — Third-Person Assertive Copula (Witnessed or Generally Known)

Used for third-person assertions that the speaker knows *from external evidence or general knowledge*:

> **ཁོང་བོད་པ་རེད** *khong Böpa red* — He/she is Tibetan (I know this — I've been told, it's evident, it's a known fact).

Using *red* for yourself sounds wrong — it implies you know your own identity from the outside, as if you were observing yourself from a distance. It would be bizarre to say "I am Tibetan (red)" because *red* is for things known externally, and you know your own identity internally.

---

#### **འདུག** *dug* — The Witnessed Existential (I Can See / Experience This Right Now)

*Dug* is the evidential existential: "it exists/is [as I can directly perceive right now]":

> **ཁ་བ་འདུག** *khawa dug* — There is snow / It is snowing (I can see the snow — right now, directly perceived).
> **ཁོང་གཉིད་སད་འདུག** *khong nyid sa dug* — He has woken up (I can see that he's awake — direct evidence before my eyes).

*Dug* carries the force of: *this is what I am perceiving at this moment with my own senses*. It is the most vivid, most immediate, most sensory of the existential forms.

---

#### **ཡོད** *yod* — First-Person Existential / Possessive

*Yod* expresses existence or possession as known from the *speaker's own perspective or inner knowledge*:

> **ང་ལ་དཔེ་ཆ་ཡོད** *ngala peche yod* — I have a book (I know I have it — it's mine, this is my situation from the inside).
> **ང་ལ་དུས་ཚོད་ཡོད** *ngala dütsö yod* — I have time (I know my own schedule; this is a first-person inner knowledge claim).

Using *yod* for someone else's possessions sounds wrong — you cannot know from the inside whether someone else has time or not. For third-person possession:

> **ཁོང་ལ་དཔེ་ཆ་ཡོད་རེད** *khong la peche yod red* — He/she has a book (I know this — reported fact, externally known; note the addition of *red*).

---

#### **མེད** *med* and **མིན** *min* — The Negative Counterparts

*Med* (non-existence, non-possession from external knowledge) and *min* (non-classification, first-person or definitional negation) mirror *yod* and *yin* in the negative.

> **ང་བོད་པ་མིན** — I am not Tibetan.
> **ཁོང་ལ་དུས་ཚོད་མེད** — He doesn't have time (externally known fact).

---

### The Egophoric Verb System

Beyond the copulas, Tibetan verbs for *actions* (as opposed to states) have an **egophoric** dimension — a grammatical marker of whether the subject is the speaker themselves and whether the action was *volitional*.

The classic example: there are different verb forms for "I went" depending on:

1. Did you *intend* to go? (volitional past) — use the *controlled* form.
2. Did you go but perhaps not fully intentionally, or are you reporting it as an observed fact about yourself? — use the *uncontrolled* or *non-egophoric* form.

And for third persons:
3. Did he go? (you are reporting from outside) — use the *evidential/non-egophoric* form.

> **ང་ཕྱིན་པ་ཡིན** *nga chin pa yin* — I went (volitional, controlled, egophoric — I chose to go and I'm reporting my own intentional action from the inside).

> **ང་སོང་འདུག** *nga song dug* — I went (non-volitional or evidential — perhaps reported as something the speaker notes about themselves from a kind of external witness perspective, or for something that happened without full intention).

For third person:
> **ཁོང་སོང་འདུག** *khong song dug* — He/she went (I can see/know this evidentially).

What is philosophically startling here is the egophoric distinction: *even for the first person*, Tibetan distinguishes between "I did this *as an intentional agent*" and "this happened to me" or "I report this about myself as an observable fact." In English we cannot grammatically distinguish "I fell" (uncontrolled) from "I fell" (equally uncontrolled), though we can add words ("I fell down accidentally" vs. "I fell to my knees voluntarily"). In Tibetan, the verb form itself marks this.

---

### The Implications for Buddhist Philosophy

It is not an accident that Tibetan is the primary vehicle of Tibetan Buddhist philosophy — a tradition deeply concerned with the nature of the self, the nature of consciousness, the relationship between observer and observed, and the distinction between *what we intend* and *what happens through us*.

A language whose grammar requires speakers to constantly mark:
- *Am I the intentional agent of this action, or did it happen through me?*
- *Am I speaking from inside my own experience, or from outside it?*
- *Am I directly perceiving this, or was I told, or do I infer it?*

...is a language extraordinarily well-suited for a philosophical tradition that asks exactly these questions. The *madhyamaka* (middle way) concern with the self as not-fully-fixed, not-fully-absent; the Dzogchen concern with the difference between awareness as subject and awareness as field; the meditational tradition's careful attention to the quality and source of knowing — all of these find natural expression in a grammar that makes these distinctions obligatory.

When a Tibetan meditation master says "there is suffering," the question of which copula they use — *dug* (I can directly perceive this), *red* (it is a known general fact), *yod* (it exists as I know from within) — is not trivial. It is a statement about the nature of their knowing and their relationship to what they know.

---

### Classical Tibetan Tense and Verb Stems

Classical Tibetan (the literary standard used for Buddhist texts) has a four-stem verb system:

| Stem | Usage |
|---|---|
| **Present** | Ongoing, habitual, or characteristically occurring action |
| **Past** | Completed action in the past |
| **Future** | Intended, expected, or potential action |
| **Imperative** | Commands |

Most classical Tibetan verbs have up to four distinct stems — not suffixes added to a single root, but four different word forms (sometimes very different, sometimes nearly identical) for each grammatical context.

> Verb "to go" (√gro):
> - Present: འགྲོ *'gro* — goes / is going.
> - Past: སོང *song* — went.
> - Future: འགྲོ *'gro* (same form as present in some verbs) — will go.
> - Imperative: སོང / ཤོག *song / shog* — go!

> Verb "to do" (√byed):
> - Present: བྱེད *byed*
> - Past: བྱས *byas*
> - Future: བྱ *bya*
> - Imperative: གྱིས *gyis*

These are suppletive forms — not derived by regular rule from a single root, but separately memorized forms. This is similar to English's "go / went" (entirely different roots) but much more systematic: *most* classical Tibetan verbs have four distinct stems.

## PART FIVE: LANGUAGES LEFT FOR A THIRD VOLUME

---

### Tamil Nadu's Neighbors: The Dravidian Panorama

**Kannada** shares Tamil's three-tense system and rational/non-rational distinction but adds a *neuter* gender class that splits the non-rational into further semantic categories. Its aspect auxiliary system differs from Tamil's in significant ways — particularly in how it handles the benefactive and the reflexive.

**Telugu** is remarkable for its *masculine/feminine/neuter* gender system in verbs — one of the few Dravidian languages to maintain a three-way gender distinction in verbal morphology. Its *honorific* system is also more elaborate than Tamil's — several levels of social deference are encoded in verb endings, more than can be covered here.

**Malayalam** (spoken in Kerala) is notable for its *neutral* treatment of rationality compared to Tamil — its gender system has been reorganized since ancient times through contact with Sanskrit.

---

### The Tibeto-Burman Panorama Beyond Tibetan

**Newari** (spoken in the Kathmandu Valley) has an evidentiality system comparable in complexity to Tibetan, with the addition of **mirative** markers — forms that grammatically mark *the speaker's surprise* at what they are witnessing or reporting.

**Ladakhi** (spoken in Ladakh, closely related to Tibetan) has an even more elaborate egophoric system than Lhasa Tibetan, with evidential distinctions that track whether knowledge comes from memory, from immediate perception, from inference, or from report.

**Burmese** has a rich system of *final particles* — clause-final particles that encode mood, evidentiality, social register, and the speaker's emotional stance, all in a single syllable at the end of the sentence.

---

### The Bantu Family

Sub-Saharan Africa's Bantu family (which includes Swahili, Zulu, Xhosa, Shona, Lingala, Kinyarwanda, Kikuyu, and hundreds more) has a feature not covered in the first volume: the **noun class system**, and its profound interaction with verb agreement.

Bantu languages typically have 8–20 noun classes (depending on the language) — far more than the three grammatical genders of most European languages. Each noun class has its own prefix on the noun and triggers *different agreement prefixes* on the verb, adjective, and other modifiers.

What makes this more than just grammatical agreement is that the noun classes carry *semantic weight*: there are classes for humans, for animals, for trees, for liquids, for abstractions, for diminutives, for augmentatives, for places, for instrument-nouns. When a word changes class, it changes meaning in a predictable way: a root in the human class means "person who does X"; in the tool class, it means "instrument for X"; in the diminutive class, it means "small or metaphorically slight version of X."

This means that Bantu verbal grammar is, at every moment, simultaneously doing *grammatical agreement* and *semantic classification* — every verb is tracking not just "who is doing what to whom" but "what kind of being is doing what kind of action to what kind of thing."

---

### The Austronesian World Beyond Tagalog

**Malagasy** (spoken in Madagascar) is Austronesian but has diverged far from the Philippine branch. It preserves a focus system but organized around three focuses: *actor*, *patient*, and *circumstantial* (covering place, time, instrument, and beneficiary together). Its word order is **VOS** — Verb-Object-Subject — one of the rarest orders among the world's languages.

**Balinese** has one of the world's most elaborate *speech level* systems — five or more registers that completely change the vocabulary (and to some extent the grammar) depending on the relative social status of speaker and listener. The "same" sentence — "Where are you going?" — is five different sentences in Balinese depending on whether you are addressing a social inferior, an equal, a Brahmin priest, or a member of the royal family.

**Hawaiian** and other Polynesian languages have simplified the Austronesian focus system dramatically but preserve aspect-based verbal grammar. Hawaiian uses **i** (past), **ke...nei** (present progressive), and **e...ana** (future/progressive) markers — a simple three-way temporal aspect system layered over a basically aspect-primary grammar.

---

### The Caucasian Languages

**Georgian** was mentioned briefly in the first volume. Its relatives deserve more:

**Chechen and Ingush** (the Nakh branch of Northeast Caucasian) have a system where *case-marking* interacts with tense and aspect in unusual ways — the case of the subject changes depending on the tense. In the present tense, volitional verbs have a nominative subject. In the past tense, the same verb takes an *ergative* subject (a case normally associated with the agent of a transitive verb). The shift in case encodes the shift in the verb's aspect: present is process (nominative), past is completed (ergative).

**Lezgian** (spoken in Dagestan and Azerbaijan) has been cited by linguists as having one of the most complex morphological systems in the world — hundreds of distinct grammatical cases, elaborate evidential distinctions, and a polypersonal verb agreement system (the verb agrees with multiple arguments simultaneously).

---

## Coda: On the Grammar of Wonder

A pattern emerges across these languages that is worth naming directly.

The languages that most elaborate the *evidential* dimension — Turkish, Tibetan, Quechua, Ojibwe — come from cultures where *the ethics of knowledge* are explicitly cultivated. In Andean culture, the evidential system is tied to a concept of community truth-telling: you share what you know, and you mark *how* you know it so that your listener can assess its reliability. To speak in Quechua without an evidential marker is not grammatically possible, but it is also *socially impossible* — you would be presenting knowledge without disclosing its source, which is a kind of deception.

In Tibetan Buddhist philosophy, the evidential copulas enact a constant mindfulness about the *quality of knowing* — whether what I am asserting is something I directly experience, something I infer, or something I was told. This is not abstract philosophy imposed on grammar; it is grammar *enacting* philosophy in every sentence.

In Tagalog, the mandatory focus system means that every transitive sentence is a statement of perspective — you must announce what this sentence is *about*, which participant you are centering. In Philippine culture, where navigating relationships and attending to others' perspectives is a deep social value (*pakikisama*, the ethic of going-along-with; *hiya*, the ethic of social shame; *kapwa*, the ethic of shared personhood), this grammatical mandatory perspective-taking may not be coincidental.

In Tamil, the aspect auxiliaries (*viṭu*, *koḷ*, *koṭu*, *vai*, *pō*, *pār*) encode an extraordinarily nuanced moral-social world: every action's completion is simultaneously tagged for *who benefited*, *whether it was done for others or for oneself*, *whether it was preparatory or final*, *whether one is open to the outcome or simply letting it go*. This is the grammar of a culture where the relationship between action and its social meaning is perpetually in view.

And in Ojibwe, the animate/inanimate distinction — which places raspberries and drums alongside humans and animals in the category of beings-in-relationship — enacts an Anishinaabe worldview in which the community of relationship extends far beyond the human. Grammar is not merely a tool for communication; it is a *practice of attention* — a repeated, moment-by-moment enactment of what the speaker understands to be real, related, and worthy of being tracked.

The ten Lakāras of Sanskrit with which we began these two volumes were not just a grammatical catalog. They were a map of the ways a speaker can stand in relation to the events of the world: seeing them unfold, receiving them from tradition, commanding them into being, wishing for them with the whole heart, or simply watching them pass. Every grammar is such a map — and no two maps of the world are quite the same.
