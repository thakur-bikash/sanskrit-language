# The Semantic Atlas
### A Dictionary of Human Meaning at Its Roots, a Theory of Language Learning, and the Five Primordial Minds

---

> *Every language is a theory of reality. To learn its roots is not to acquire vocabulary — it is to inhabit a different ontology.*

---

## Preface: Why Roots, Not Words

The standard dictionary translates words. This atlas translates **the structures beneath words** — the roots, image schemas, semantic primes, and thematic roles that every language uses to carve reality into speakable form.

Translation conflicts — especially philosophical ones — do not arise because people use different words for the same thing. They arise because languages *force their speakers to decide different things*. Japanese requires you to declare whether a being is animate or inanimate before you can say it exists. Spanish requires you to decide whether a quality is essential or contingent before you can predicate it of anything. Sanskrit requires you to declare, in every verb, whether the action benefits the doer or another. English requires you to identify an agent causing an effect on a patient in almost every sentence.

These are not stylistic choices. They are grammatical obligations — and they shape what their speakers can easily think, what they must labor to express, and what philosophical positions feel natural or strange to them.

This atlas is built on four analytical layers, drawn from the documents compiled across Sanskrit dhātu studies, comparative root linguistics, NSM theory, image schema analysis, and thematic role research:

**Layer 1 — NSM Primes:** The ~65 semantic primes identified by Wierzbicka that cannot be defined using simpler terms in any language. These are the true atoms of human meaning — the translation-proof bedrock.

**Layer 2 — Image Schemas:** The ~12 embodied spatial structures (Source-Path-Goal, Containment, Force-Dynamics, etc.) that languages use to physically ground abstract meaning. Grammar is largely these schemas made obligatory.

**Layer 3 — Verb Classes:** Dixon's ~20 universal semantic domains (Motion, Cognition, Emotion, Existence, Possession, etc.) that every language must cover — but carves differently.

**Layer 4 — Thematic Roles:** The dramatic roles (Agent, Experiencer, Patient, Mover, Source, Recipient) that every sentence distributes among its participants. The same event, staged with different roles, implies a different theory of causation and responsibility.

# Part One: The Dictionary

## How to Read an Entry

Each entry is organized as follows:

- **Concept name** — the universal semantic function being encoded
- **NSM prime(s)** — the bedrock atoms this concept is built from
- **Image schema** — the embodied spatial structure grounding the abstraction
- **Thematic role** — how the language stages the drama of this concept
- **Five language entries**, each containing:
  - *Root(s)* — the irreducible verbal seed(s)
  - *Form* — a representative inflected word
  - *Gloss* — literal translation of the form
  - *Metaphor* — the spatial/bodily image grounding the concept in this language
  - *Philosophical key* — what this encoding reveals about the language's theory of reality

## Entry 1: TO EXIST

**NSM Prime:** THERE IS  
**Image Schema:** LOCATIVE PRESENCE / BALANCE  
**Thematic Role:** EXISTENTIAL

---

**Sanskrit — √as · √bhū**  
*Form:* asti · bhavati  
*Gloss:* is · becomes-is  
*Metaphor:* Existence as continuous action — even being is a doing  
*Philosophical key:* Sanskrit splits bare existence (√as — "to be, to throw") from becoming-existence (√bhū — "to become, to arise from below"). Static being is philosophically secondary; processual becoming is primary. Even the word for "world" (bhava) and "earth" (bhūmi) come from √bhū — the world is that which is always in the act of arising. Nothing merely *is*; everything is *being-toward*.

---

**Japanese — iru · aru**  
*Form:* います · あります  
*Gloss:* animate-dwell · inanimate-be-present  
*Metaphor:* Existence as ontologically typed dwelling — what you are determines how you be  
*Philosophical key:* The mandatory iru/aru split means that every existence-claim in Japanese is simultaneously an ontological classification. Dogs, people, and feelings use iru (animate); books, money, and time use aru (inanimate). You cannot think of anything existing without first deciding what kind of thing is existing. This is not philosophy — it is grammar.

---

**Chinese — 是 shì · 在 zài · 有 yǒu**  
*Form:* shì / zài / yǒu  
*Gloss:* identity-be · located-be · possessive-there-is  
*Metaphor:* Existence as relational positioning — to be is always to be somewhere in relation to something  
*Philosophical key:* Chinese demands three verbs where English uses one. Identity (是: "she is a teacher"), location (在: "she is in Beijing"), and presence (有: "there is a problem") are grammatically distinct modes of being. Existence is inherently positional and relational — it always asks: what class does this belong to, where is it, and who has it?

---

**English — be (PIE \*bheu-)**  
*Form:* is · am · are  
*Gloss:* be  
*Metaphor:* Existence as undifferentiated state — being is being is being  
*Philosophical key:* English collapses identity, location, quality, and existence into a single verb. "She is beautiful" (identity), "she is here" (location), "there is a problem" (existence) — all "be." This ontological flatness enables radical abstraction and universalizing claims ("all men are mortal") but erases the grades and textures of being that other languages consider philosophically essential. It is the grammatical foundation of logical universalism.

---

**Spanish — ser < esse · estar < stare**  
*Form:* es · está  
*Gloss:* essentially-is · contingently-stands  
*Metaphor:* Existence as ontological spectrum — being has grades from pure essence to temporary accident  
*Philosophical key:* Every Spanish sentence containing "be" requires a metaphysical commitment. Ser (from Latin esse — pure being) marks what something is by nature or definition. Estar (from stare — to stand) marks what it is currently doing, feeling, or where it happens to be. Spanish speakers live in a world where being has grades — and every utterance must declare which grade applies. The ser/estar split is, grammatically speaking, the distinction between essence and existence that anchored five centuries of Spanish scholastic theology.

## Entry 2: TO KNOW

**NSM Prime:** KNOW  
**Image Schema:** CONTAINMENT / LIGHT  
**Thematic Role:** EXPERIENCER

---

**Sanskrit — √jñā · √vid**  
*Form:* jānāti · vetti  
*Gloss:* knows-propositionally · knows-by-direct-witness  
*Metaphor:* Knowledge as illumination (jñā) or as direct witness by having seen (vid)  
*Philosophical key:* √jñā (cognate with Greek gnosis, Latin cognoscere) is inferential, propositional knowledge — knowing that. √vid (cognate with Latin video and the Vedas themselves) is direct experiential knowledge — knowing by having seen or experienced directly. The Vedas are śruti, "that which is heard" — revelation as direct auditory witness, not inference. The entire Vedantic tradition hinges on this distinction: jñāna-yoga (path of knowledge) aims not at information but at direct experiential recognition of Brahman.

---

**Japanese — 知る shiru · わかる wakaru**  
*Form:* 知っている · わかった  
*Gloss:* know-by-accumulated-information · came-apart-clearly (= understood)  
*Metaphor:* Understanding as the act of separating and distinguishing — things become clear when they come apart  
*Philosophical key:* Wakaru (to understand, to become clear) shares its root with waku (to divide, to separate). To understand is for something to "come apart clearly" in your mind — to resolve into its distinct components. Confusion is things being fused together; understanding is things separating into clarity. The Japanese model of comprehension is analytic and spatial: you understand when you can divide.

---

**Chinese — 知 zhī · 懂 dǒng · 了解 liǎojiě**  
*Form:* 知道 zhīdào · 懂得 dǒngde  
*Gloss:* know-the-way · understand-obtain  
*Metaphor:* 思 = field 田 + heart 心 — knowledge grows in the field of the heart  
*Philosophical key:* In Chinese, knowing the way (知道, zhīdào — literally "know-Dao") means knowing the proper path through reality, not merely knowing facts. The organ of cognition is 心 (xīn — heart-mind): all thinking is heart-work. To understand (懂) suggests a kind of hearing-through or settling. Chinese cognition has no Cartesian split — thought and feeling grow in the same organ, the heart, and knowledge always has a relational, moral dimension.

---

**English — know (two collapsed OE words: witan + cunnan)**  
*Form:* know  
*Gloss:* know  
*Metaphor:* Understanding as standing beneath — "under-stand" means to position yourself beneath something, supporting it from below  
*Philosophical key:* Old English once had two distinct knowing verbs: witan (to know facts — source of "wit") and cunnan (to know how to do — source of "can"). These collapsed into "know," losing the distinction that Romance languages preserved (savoir/connaître, saber/conocer). "Understand" preserves an archaic spatial metaphor: to stand beneath something so as to hold it up and grasp it from below. English knowing is architecturally and agentically structured — the knower is a foundation.

---

**Spanish — saber < sapere · conocer < cognoscere**  
*Form:* sabe · conoce  
*Gloss:* knows-propositionally · knows-by-acquaintance  
*Metaphor:* Saber as savoring — sapere means both "to know" and "to taste"; wisdom is knowledge that has been tasted  
*Philosophical key:* Saber (from sapere — to taste, to be wise) is propositional: knowing facts, knowing how to do things, knowing that something is true. Conocer (from cognoscere — to know thoroughly) is relational: knowing a person, a city, a text intimately, from the inside. Spanish thus preserves the knowing-that vs. knowing-who split that English collapsed. The Spanish word for "flavor" (sabor) shares its root with "knowing" (saber) — wisdom is what you have tasted and digested.

---

## Entry 3: TO UNDERSTAND

**NSM Prime:** KNOW · THINK  
**Image Schema:** CONTAINMENT / UP-DOWN / SEPARATION  
**Thematic Role:** EXPERIENCER

---

**Sanskrit — √budh**  
*Form:* budhyate  
*Gloss:* awakens · perceives · becomes-aware  
*Metaphor:* Understanding as awakening — ignorance is sleep, understanding is emergence into wakefulness  
*Philosophical key:* √budh means both "to understand" and "to wake up." The Buddha is literally "the one who has awoken." In Sanskrit epistemology, ignorance (avidyā — non-seeing) is a kind of sleep or obscuration. Understanding is not acquiring information — it is emerging from a dreamlike state into clear perception. This is why Indian philosophy consistently presents liberation (mokṣa) in terms of waking up, not learning. To understand the deepest truths is to stop dreaming.

---

**Japanese — わかる wakaru**  
*Form:* わかった  
*Gloss:* it-came-apart · it-became-clear  
*Metaphor:* Understanding as successful separation of previously fused elements  
*Philosophical key:* Wakaru (to understand) shares its root with waku (to divide) and wakeru (to separate). To understand is for the matter to "come apart clearly" — for what was confused and fused to resolve into distinguishable components. Confusion (konran) is things tangled together; understanding is clean separation. This is diametrically opposite to the Spanish model of comprehension (grasping together) and the Chinese model (full illumination). The Japanese mind understands by analysis; the Spanish mind understands by synthesis; the Chinese mind understands by radiance.

---

**Chinese — 明白 míngbái · 懂 dǒng**  
*Form:* 明白了  
*Gloss:* bright-white-completed  
*Metaphor:* 明 = 日 sun + 月 moon — clarity is the co-presence of both lights simultaneously  
*Philosophical key:* The primary Chinese expression for understanding, 明白 (míngbái), is composed of 明 (bright = sun + moon together) and 白 (white/pure). To understand is to be in a state of complete luminous presence — where both solar and lunar illumination are active at once. Understanding is not separation (Japanese) or grasping (Spanish) but total, simultaneous radiance. This reflects the Chinese aesthetic of comprehension as wholeness rather than analysis.

---

**English — understand (< understandan)**  
*Form:* understand  
*Gloss:* stand-beneath  
*Metaphor:* Comprehension as supporting something from below — to understand is to be the foundation  
*Philosophical key:* Old English understandan meant to stand in the midst of, or beneath, something so as to support it and grasp it from below. The knower is structural — they are what holds the understood thing up. This spatial logic pervades English cognition: we "grasp" concepts, "hold" beliefs, "build" arguments, "construct" theories. Knowledge is architecture and the knower is the builder.

---

**Spanish — entender < intendere · comprender < comprehendere**  
*Form:* entiende · comprende  
*Gloss:* stretches-toward · grasps-together  
*Metaphor:* Understanding as physical seizure — you reach out and close your hand around an idea  
*Philosophical key:* Comprender (com + prehendere — to grasp together) treats understanding as a physical act of simultaneous closure: you reach out and enclose the idea, bringing its parts together in your grasp. Entender (in + tendere — to stretch toward) is the prior movement of reaching out. Understanding requires both the extension and the closure. The Spanish model of comprehension is synthetic and corporeal — you must first stretch, then seize.

---

## Entry 4: TO BECOME

**NSM Prime:** HAPPEN · BECOME  
**Image Schema:** TRANSFORMATION / SOURCE-PATH-GOAL  
**Thematic Role:** PATIENT-MOVER (undergoing while self-propelling)

---

**Sanskrit — √bhū · √jan**  
*Form:* bhavati · jāyate  
*Gloss:* becomes · is-born  
*Metaphor:* Becoming as the primary mode of existence — static being is derived from becoming, not the other way  
*Philosophical key:* √bhū (to become, to arise) is more ontologically fundamental than √as (to merely be). Reality is processual: static existence is a derivative and temporary stabilization within endless becoming. Bhāva (existence, emotion, becoming), bhūmi (earth), bhūta (element, what has become), bhava (the world) — all from √bhū. Even the concept of "element" in Sanskrit is "that which has already undergone becoming." This root generates all of Sanskrit metaphysics.

---

**Japanese — なる naru**  
*Form:* なった  
*Gloss:* became · arose · sounded  
*Metaphor:* Becoming as intransitive self-arising — the highest things naru, they are not made to be  
*Philosophical key:* Naru is the intransitive counterpart of suru (to do, to make). The distinction is not merely grammatical — it is aesthetic and spiritual. Things that naru (arise organically) are considered superior to things that are suru-ed (forced into existence). Great art naru-s into being; forced art is suru-ed. A skilled tea ceremony master creates an atmosphere that naru-s. This preference for organic arising over active forcing pervades Japanese aesthetics, ethics (wu wei imported via Zen), and the ideal of effortless mastery.

---

**Chinese — 成 chéng · 变 biàn**  
*Form:* 成了 · 变了  
*Gloss:* become-complete · transform  
*Metaphor:* 变 = silk 糸 unwinding — change is like thread releasing itself  
*Philosophical key:* Chinese distinguishes 成 (chéng — to become by completing, to succeed in becoming a final form) from 变 (biàn — to change, transform). 成 implies teleological becoming — arriving at a proper form. 变 implies continuous transformation without necessary endpoint, like silk thread unwinding. The I Ching (易经, Book of Changes) is built on 变 — the universe is constant transformation. One character is about arriving; the other is about the ongoing act of changing.

---

**English — become (be + come)**  
*Form:* become  
*Gloss:* come-to-be  
*Metaphor:* Becoming as spatial arrival — new states are destinations you travel to  
*Philosophical key:* "Become" encodes becoming as a journey completed — you arrive at a new state the way you arrive at a place. Yet English grammatically prefers static "be" to dynamic "become" in most contexts. The language possesses a becoming verb but uses it relatively rarely, defaulting to static predication ("she is anxious" rather than "she became anxious"). English leans ontologically toward states rather than processes — toward being rather than becoming.

---

**Spanish — volverse · ponerse · hacerse · llegar a ser**  
*Form:* se volvió / se puso / se hizo / llegó a ser  
*Gloss:* turned-itself / put-itself / made-itself / arrived-at-being  
*Metaphor:* Four degrees of becoming, each encoding a different theory of how change happens  
*Philosophical key:* Spanish has four distinct "become" verbs, each encoding a different causal structure: volverse (sudden, involuntary change of deep character: "se volvió loco" — he went mad), ponerse (temporary emotional or physical state: "se puso triste" — she became sad for now), hacerse (gradual intentional transformation through effort: "se hizo médico" — she worked to become a doctor), llegar a ser (long-term cumulative achievement: "llegó a ser presidente" — he eventually became president). Every act of becoming in Spanish requires a theory of how it happened.

## Entry 5: TO FEEL / EMOTION

**NSM Prime:** FEEL  
**Image Schema:** CONTAINMENT / FORCE-DYNAMICS  
**Thematic Role:** EXPERIENCER

---

**Sanskrit — √bhāv · √ved · rasa**  
*Form:* bhāvayati  
*Gloss:* causes-to-become · makes-felt  
*Metaphor:* Emotion as becoming — feelings are not states you have but states you are temporarily in the act of becoming  
*Philosophical key:* Sanskrit's emotional vocabulary culminates in the rasa system — nine fundamental aesthetic emotions (śṛṅgāra, hāsya, karuṇa, raudra, vīra, bhayānaka, bībhatsa, adbhuta, śānta) that are not merely personal feelings but cosmic flavors or essences (rasa literally means "taste, sap, juice"). Emotion is not private subjective state but participation in a shared aesthetic-cosmic register. To feel grief (karuṇa) is to align oneself with the grief-essence of the universe. Feeling is ontological participation.

---

**Japanese — 感じる kanjiru · 気持ち kimochi**  
*Form:* 感じた  
*Gloss:* sense-received · spirit-hold  
*Metaphor:* Emotion as textured sensory reception — feelings have granular physical textures before they have names  
*Philosophical key:* Japanese emotional vocabulary is uniquely dense with gitaigo (phenomimes): doki-doki (heart pounding with anticipation or anxiety), shiku-shiku (quiet, persistent, low-level weeping), waku-waku (excited anticipation rising from within), biku-biku (flinching with anxiety). These are not metaphors — they are primary encodings of pre-verbal feeling textures. Kimochi (feeling/mood) is 気 (ki — life-energy, spirit) + 持ち (mochi — holding): a feeling is the quality of what your spirit is currently holding.

---

**Chinese — 感 gǎn · 情 qíng**  
*Form:* 感情  
*Gloss:* sense-move · feeling-condition  
*Metaphor:* 感 = heart 心 in motion — emotion is literally the movement of the heart-mind  
*Philosophical key:* In Chinese, 感 (gǎn — to feel, to sense, to be moved) places 心 (heart/mind) in motion. 情 (qíng — feeling, emotion, human affection) denotes the ongoing condition of the heart. All emotion in Chinese is heart-motion or heart-condition. Since 心 is simultaneously the organ of thought and the organ of feeling, Chinese has no structural separation between cognition and emotion — they are conditions of the same organ. A person who thinks clearly also feels clearly; a person whose heart is disturbed cannot think well.

---

**English — feel (< Proto-Germanic, related to palm of hand)**  
*Form:* feel  
*Gloss:* feel  
*Metaphor:* Emotion as tactile contact — to feel something is to touch it with the palm of awareness  
*Philosophical key:* The Proto-Germanic root of "feel" is related to the palm of the hand (palpon). To feel emotions is etymologically to touch them — the primary sense is haptic. This is why English speaks of being "struck" by beauty, being "touched" by kindness, "grasping" a feeling, being "moved" (motion as cause of emotion). The entire landscape of English inner life is structured as a physical surface that the agent-self navigates and contacts. Emotions arrive as forces that make contact with the self.

---

**Spanish — sentir < sentire**  
*Form:* siente · sintió  
*Gloss:* senses · felt  
*Metaphor:* One root for sensation, emotion, and regret — sorrow is literally feeling-again  
*Philosophical key:* Sentir covers the entire spectrum from physical sensation (siento el frío — I feel the cold), to emotion (siento amor — I feel love), to moral pain and regret (lo siento — I feel it = I am sorry). The same root covers the body's sensing, the soul's feeling, and the ethical faculty's recognition of wrong. In Spanish, an apology is literally "I am feeling it" — sorrow is not a separate moral category from sensation, it is sensation at its most morally charged register.

## Entry 6: TO SPEAK

**NSM Prime:** SAY · WORDS  
**Image Schema:** LINK / SOURCE-PATH-GOAL  
**Thematic Role:** AGENT → RECIPIENT

---

**Sanskrit — √vac · √vad · √bhāṣ**  
*Form:* vacati · vadati · bhāṣate  
*Gloss:* speaks-sacredly · says-plainly · speaks-articulately  
*Metaphor:* Speech as cosmic creative power — to name is to participate in the creation of the world  
*Philosophical key:* Sanskrit has 229 speech roots organized by the moral quality of the speech act — praising, reviling, proclaiming, narrating secretly, counseling carefully, informing formally, mocking gently. Vāc (speech, from √vac) is a goddess — the creative power of the universe. The Rig Veda opens: "In the beginning was Word." To speak in Sanskrit is not neutral communication — it is a moral and ontological act. Every speech act participates in the sacred structure of reality or violates it.

---

**Japanese — 言う iu · 話す hanasu · 語る kataru**  
*Form:* 言った · 話した · 語った  
*Gloss:* said · spoke/talked · narrated  
*Metaphor:* Words carry living spirit — kotodama (言霊) — speech releases soul into the world  
*Philosophical key:* Kotodama (言霊 — word-spirit) is the Japanese understanding that words carry living spiritual power. To speak is not to transmit information — it is to release spirit into the world. This is why ritual language (norito — Shinto prayers), poetic language (waka, haiku), and apologies carry such extraordinary weight in Japanese culture. Each utterance is simultaneously a spiritual act. Words must be chosen with care not because they might be misunderstood but because they are alive.

---

**Chinese — 说 shuō · 言 yán · 讲 jiǎng**  
*Form:* 说话 shuōhuà  
*Gloss:* say-speech  
*Metaphor:* Proper naming as social-cosmic governance — zhengming (正名, rectification of names)  
*Philosophical key:* Confucian zhengming (正名 — rectification of names) holds that correct language maintains social and cosmic order: "When names are not correct, speech is not orderly. When speech is not orderly, affairs cannot be accomplished." Chinese links the quality of language directly to the quality of civilization. Wrong words — calling a servant a minister, a bad act a good one — cause social disintegration. Language is not communication but cosmological governance.

---

**English — speak · say · tell**  
*Form:* speak · say · tell  
*Gloss:* speak (manner) · say (content) · tell (audience)  
*Metaphor:* Speech as information transfer between agent and recipient  
*Philosophical key:* English maintains the speak/say/tell distinction (manner of speaking vs. content of message vs. targeting an audience), but has largely lost the moral gradation that Sanskrit encoded across 229 roots. The language does not grammaticalize whether speech is sacred or profane, praising or reviling — these become vocabulary choices, not grammatical obligations. English speech is pragmatically efficient and morally neutral at the structural level.

---

**Spanish — hablar < fabulare · decir < dicere**  
*Form:* habla · dice  
*Gloss:* speaks (narratively) · says (authoritatively)  
*Metaphor:* Hablar from fabula — all speaking is implicitly storytelling  
*Philosophical key:* Hablar comes from fabulare — to tell fables, to speak narratively. Every act of speaking in Spanish is implicitly a story being told. Decir (to say, to state) carries more propositional weight and authority. The subjunctive mood marks all reported speech that the speaker does not commit to: "me dijo que fuera" (he told me to go — the subjunctive signals this is his report, not my reality). Spanish encodes the epistemic status of every quoted thought.

---

## Entry 7: TO WANT / DESIRE

**NSM Prime:** WANT  
**Image Schema:** SOURCE-PATH-GOAL / FORCE  
**Thematic Role:** AGENT

---

**Sanskrit — √iṣ · √kāmu · √vāñch**  
*Form:* icchati · kāmayate · vāñchati  
*Gloss:* desires · loves-desires · longs-for  
*Metaphor:* Desire as the primordial cosmic force — Kāma is the first movement of creation  
*Philosophical key:* √kāmu (desire, love — Kāma is the god of desire) connects personal wanting to cosmic eros. The Atharva Veda and the Rig Veda both locate desire (kāma) as the first principle of creation: "Desire arose in the beginning — that was the primal seed of mind." Wanting is cosmic before it is personal. The Kāmasūtra addresses not sexual technique but the entire philosophy of desire as the force that moves consciousness through life. To want is to participate in the generative pulse of the universe.

---

**Japanese — ほしい hoshii · したい shitai**  
*Form:* ほしい · したい  
*Gloss:* want-thing · want-to-do  
*Metaphor:* Desire split by grammatical object type — you must know what kind of desire you are having  
*Philosophical key:* Japanese encodes two grammatically distinct desire structures that cannot be confused: hoshii (wanting a noun — a thing, an object) and shitai (wanting to do a verb — an action, a performance). Before you can express desire in Japanese you must already know whether your desire is acquisitive (directed at having) or performative (directed at doing). This is not philosophy — it is mandatory grammar. The distinction resembles the Aristotelian difference between appetitive desire and deliberative desire.

---

**Chinese — 想 xiǎng · 要 yào · 欲 yù**  
*Form:* 想要 xiǎngyào  
*Gloss:* think-want  
*Metaphor:* 要 merges need and want — necessity and desire share a grammatical house  
*Philosophical key:* 要 (yào) covers both "need" and "want" — the distinction between necessity and desire is contextual, not grammaticalized. This collapses what both Stoic philosophy and Kantian ethics consider a crucial ethical distinction. 欲 (yù — desire, lust) is the ancient character preserved in Buddhist vocabulary as the dangerous form of wanting — the kind that generates karma. Chinese philosophical tradition (especially Buddhism and Daoism) is deeply concerned with the right relationship to 欲 and largely advocates its reduction.

---

**English — want (< Old Norse vanta — to lack)**  
*Form:* want  
*Gloss:* want  
*Metaphor:* Desire as lack — to want is to be without; desire is defined by an absence  
*Philosophical key:* The English word "want" comes from a root meaning "to lack, to be without." To want something is to be in a state of absence regarding it. This encodes a fundamentally negative and privative model of desire: desire is emptiness that seeks to be filled. Compare Sanskrit kāma, where desire is positive creative force — not a hole but a generative pulse. The English conception of desire as lack anticipates Schopenhauer and Lacanian lack; the Sanskrit conception anticipates Tantric philosophy and Bergson's élan vital.

---

**Spanish — querer < quaerere**  
*Form:* quiere  
*Gloss:* wants · loves · seeks · quests  
*Metaphor:* Desire and love are the same root — all wanting is ultimately a form of questing  
*Philosophical key:* Querer covers both "to want" and "to love." Te quiero means both "I want you" and "I love you." This fusion is philosophically fundamental: in Spanish, love is not categorically separate from desire — it is desire at its most devoted. All love is a form of questing (quaerere — to seek). Quijote's impossible quest is built from this root: he quiere Dulcinea the way a saint loves God — desire raised to devotion raised to madness. In Spanish, love and will share a root.

---

## Entry 8: TO MOVE / GO

**NSM Prime:** MOVE  
**Image Schema:** SOURCE-PATH-GOAL  
**Thematic Role:** MOVER / AGENT

---

**Sanskrit — √gam · √yā · √car · √tsar · √cup**  
*Form:* gacchati · yāti · carati · tsarati · cupati  
*Gloss:* goes · travels-steadily · wanders-grazes · goes-secretly · moves-slowly-with-difficulty  
*Metaphor:* Motion as moral disclosure — how you move reveals who you are  
*Philosophical key:* Sanskrit has approximately 350 distinct motion roots encoding not just direction and speed but the moral and qualitative character of the movement. √ak (to move crookedly), √cup (to move slowly with visible difficulty), √tsar (to move deceitfully, secretly), √at (to move continuously without stopping), √krand (to move while crying out). The moral and physical dimensions of motion are grammatically inseparable. Dharmic walking is not the same action as adharmic walking — they are different verbs.

---

**Japanese — 行く iku · 来る kuru**  
*Form:* 行った · 来た  
*Gloss:* went-away · came-here  
*Metaphor:* All motion is speaker-anchored — the human body is the absolute origin of every spatial reference  
*Philosophical key:* Japanese encodes the speaker's position into every motion verb — iku (going away from the speaker) vs. kuru (coming toward the speaker). Combined with directional auxiliaries (te iku = going-direction, te kuru = coming-direction), every movement in Japanese is triangulated against a human center. Motion is never abstract — it is always relational to the present body. The speaker is the absolute reference point of the universe of motion. This is not ego-centrism but phenomenological precision.

---

**Chinese — 去 qù · 来 lái · resultative complements**  
*Form:* 进来 jìn-lái · 出去 chū-qù  
*Gloss:* enter-come · exit-go  
*Metaphor:* Motion as compositional assembly — direction is a module added to base movement  
*Philosophical key:* Chinese encodes motion direction through modular resultative complement constructions. Any direction can be specified by combining a base motion verb with a directional component: 上来 (ascend-come = come up), 下去 (descend-go = go down), 进去 (enter-go = go in), 出来 (exit-come = come out). This is a combinatorial, compositional system — motion is assembled from parts. The result is that any spatial trajectory can be precisely specified by selecting the right combination of modules.

---

**English — go · come · move (+ phrasal verbs)**  
*Form:* go · come · move · get through · fall apart  
*Gloss:* directional motion + abstract metaphorical motion  
*Metaphor:* Motion as the master metaphor for all abstract experience — to live is to move through a landscape of states  
*Philosophical key:* English encodes all abstract inner experience through spatial motion metaphors: "get through" difficulty, "break down" under pressure, "rise above" circumstances, "fall apart" emotionally, "move on" from grief, "come around" to an idea, "push through" resistance. The entire landscape of mental and emotional life is a physical landscape through which an agent-self moves. To make progress is to move forward. To regress is to move back. The English self is essentially a traveler.

---

**Spanish — ir < ire · venir < venire**  
*Form:* va · viene  
*Gloss:* goes · comes  
*Metaphor:* Motion as change of ontological situation — to go is to leave what you were; to come is to arrive at what you will be  
*Philosophical key:* Spanish preserves the ancient Latin sense of motion as ontological transition. Ir implies departure from one's current state or location — it involves a leave-taking. Venir implies arrival into the frame of the current speaker or situation. Crucially, ir a + infinitive (voy a decir — I am going to say) encodes the future as motion toward — the future is a place you travel to. Time is spatialized as a path, and the Spanish self moves through it as an agent.

---

## Entry 9: TO LOVE

**NSM Prime:** WANT · FEEL  
**Image Schema:** LINK / CONTAINMENT  
**Thematic Role:** EXPERIENCER / AGENT

---

**Sanskrit — √prī · √kāmu · √sneha**  
*Form:* prīyate · kāmayate · snihyati  
*Gloss:* is-delighted · desires · is-adhesive-toward  
*Metaphor:* Three metaphysical theories of love: as pleasure, as cosmic force, as viscous material bond  
*Philosophical key:* Sanskrit encodes love through three distinct roots encoding different ontologies: √prī (love as delight and satisfaction — prīti), √kāmu (love as cosmic erotic force — kāma, desire as the engine of creation and rebirth), √sneha (love as oil or viscosity — literally: the beloved clings to you as ghee clings to a vessel). Sanskrit love is not one thing but three irreducibly different relationships to the other — hedonic, cosmological, and material.

---

**Japanese — 愛 ai · 好き suki · 恋 koi**  
*Form:* 愛する · 好きだ · 恋する  
*Gloss:* love-universal · like-love · romantic-longing  
*Metaphor:* Three registers: universal love, preference-affection, and the ache of romantic longing  
*Philosophical key:* Ai (愛) is deep universal or parental love — the love that transcends preference. Suki (好き) is lighter affection, preference, or everyday love — "I love coffee" uses suki, not ai. Koi (恋) is specifically romantic love characterized by its ache, its longing, and the awareness that it may not be fulfilled — the cherry blossom feeling applied to persons. These are not synonyms or degrees of the same thing. They are categorically different relationships whose confusion would be a social and philosophical error.

---

**Chinese — 爱 ài · 喜欢 xǐhuān · 缘分 yuánfèn**  
*Form:* 爱你 · 喜欢你  
*Gloss:* love-you · like-enjoy-you  
*Metaphor:* Love as fulfillment of cosmic pre-arrangement — yuánfèn (缘分, fated portion)  
*Philosophical key:* Chinese encodes love within the concept of yuánfèn (缘分 — fate-portion, predestined karmic connection): the cosmological arrangement that explains why two specific people find each other. Love is not random attraction but the completion of a thread (缘, yuán — literally thread/connection) that was woven before this life. Romantic love in Chinese is participation in a larger cosmic order. To love someone is to recognize the pre-existing thread between you.

---

**English — love · like**  
*Form:* love · like  
*Gloss:* love · like  
*Metaphor:* A spectrum from mild preference to ultimate devotion, compressed into two words  
*Philosophical key:* English has lost the distinctions that Greek (eros, agape, philia, storge), Sanskrit (prī, kāmu, sneha), and Japanese (ai, suki, koi) maintain. "Love" must span: "I love pizza," "I love my mother," "I love you utterly and absolutely." This creates permanent contextual disambiguation labor — the listener must always determine what kind of love is meant. The compression also enables a kind of universalism: the single word "love" can be predicated of anything, making it available as a universal ethical and spiritual category.

---

**Spanish — amar < amare · querer < quaerere**  
*Form:* ama · quiere  
*Gloss:* loves-devotedly · loves-desires  
*Metaphor:* Two theories of love: permanent devotion (amar) and active seeking/wanting (querer)  
*Philosophical key:* Amar (from amare) is the love of commitment, permanence, and formal devotion — used for deep lasting love and in formal/literary contexts. Querer (from quaerere — to seek, to quest) is the love that contains desire, will, and active wanting — warmer, more daily, and more explicit about the element of longing. Te amo is the love of permanence; te quiero is the love that still wants. Spanish love is always either settled devotion or active questing — and the grammar forces you to know which.

## Entry 10: TO DO / ACT

**NSM Prime:** DO  
**Image Schema:** FORCE / CAUSATION  
**Thematic Role:** AGENT

---

**Sanskrit — √kṛ**  
*Form:* karoti  
*Gloss:* does · makes · acts  
*Metaphor:* Action as the generative ontological principle — the universe is not a collection of things but a web of actions  
*Philosophical key:* √kṛ is the most frequently occurring dhātu in the Sanskrit corpus. From it: karma (action as cosmic law and consequence), kāraṇa (cause), kārya (that which is to be done — duty as action), kriyā (ritual action, yogic action), kārin (agent), kṛti (a doing, a work). Action is not incidental to reality — it IS the structure of reality. Sanskrit ontology is a theory of action. The Bhagavad Gita is entirely a meditation on the nature of this root: what is right action (karma), who is the true agent (kartā), and what is the action's proper goal.

---

**Japanese — する suru · なる naru**  
*Form:* する · なった  
*Gloss:* does/makes-actively · became-organically  
*Metaphor:* The great Japanese distinction: forced action vs. organic arising  
*Philosophical key:* Suru (intentional doing, imposing will on the world) and naru (organic arising, becoming without forcing) represent two irreducibly different modes of causation in Japanese consciousness. The highest aesthetic and ethical ideals consistently favor naru over suru. The master calligrapher's brush moves as if the stroke naru-s rather than is suru-ed. Excellence conceals its effort. Zen's concept of mushin (no-mind action) is the state in which suru-ing disappears into naru-ing — the agent dissolves into the act.

---

**Chinese — 做 zuò · 为 wéi**  
*Form:* 做 · 无为  
*Gloss:* do-make · act/be — and its negation: non-acting  
*Metaphor:* 为 = both "to act" and "to be for" — action is inseparable from purpose  
*Philosophical key:* 为 (wéi) covers "to act," "to be," and "for the sake of." Its negation — wu wei (无为 — non-acting) — is the central concept of Daoism: not passive inaction but the art of acting in alignment with the natural order such that no forced, artificial action is necessary. Chinese philosophical tradition is built on interrogating the nature of this root: what is the proper relationship between human action and the natural order of the Dao? The answer — wu wei — is encoded in the negation of the most basic action word.

---

**English — do · make**  
*Form:* do · make  
*Gloss:* perform-action · create  
*Metaphor:* Two distinct theories of action: performance (do) vs. fabrication (make)  
*Philosophical key:* English uniquely preserves the do/make distinction (from Latin agere/facere): do is process-oriented (performing an action — do homework, do a dance), make is product-oriented (creating something — make a table, make noise). This encodes two fundamentally different theories of action: one where the action IS the point (do), and one where the product IS the point (make). The distinction roughly tracks the Aristotelian difference between praxis (action done for its own sake) and poiesis (action done to produce something).

---

**Spanish — hacer < facere**  
*Form:* hace  
*Gloss:* does · makes · creates  
*Metaphor:* One root covers doing and making — creation and performance are the same act  
*Philosophical key:* Spanish collapses English's do/make into hacer. Hacer los deberes (do homework), hacer una mesa (make a table), hacer ruido (make noise). Creation and performance are one act. What you do, you make; what you make, you do. This reflects the Latin facere — which also gives English "fact" (factum — what has been made), "factory," "feature," "fashion," "fiction." In Spanish, a fact is literally "a thing that has been done/made." Reality is the accumulated making of agents.

# Part Two: The Learning Theory

## The Semantic Genesis Method
### Building Language from the Ground Up

This is not immersion. It is not grammar memorization. It is the deliberate construction of a language from its deepest generative layers outward — the way a geologist reads rock strata from bedrock to surface, or the way an embryo builds an organism from the most fundamental structures toward the most complex.

The central claim: **grammar rules feel arbitrary because they are taught before the conceptual work they do is understood.** Once you understand the conceptual work, the grammar rule feels not just necessary but inevitable.

---

### Phase 1 — The 65 Seeds (NSM Primes)

**What:** The ~65 semantic primes identified by Wierzbicka — the irreducible atoms of human meaning that cannot be defined using simpler terms in any language. They are the foundation of translation precisely because they exist in every language.

**The primes, grouped:**

*Self and Other:* I · YOU · SOMEONE · PEOPLE · BODY  
*Mental States:* THINK · KNOW · WANT · FEEL · SEE · HEAR  
*Speech:* SAY · WORDS · TRUE  
*Action:* DO · HAPPEN · MOVE  
*Existence:* THERE IS · HAVE · LIVE · DIE  
*Evaluation:* GOOD · BAD  
*Descriptors:* BIG · SMALL · THE SAME · OTHER  
*Time:* WHEN · NOW · BEFORE · AFTER · A LONG TIME · A SHORT TIME  
*Space:* WHERE · HERE · ABOVE · BELOW · FAR · NEAR · SIDE  
*Logic:* NOT · MAYBE · CAN · BECAUSE · IF  
*Similarity:* LIKE / AS / WAY  

**How to learn them:** Not through translation. Through pointing, demonstration, and immediate use in the target language. The primes are learned simultaneously across all target languages — not one language at a time. You learn what KNOW sounds like in Sanskrit, Japanese, Chinese, English, and Spanish in the same sitting, before learning any derived vocabulary.

**Why this first:** Because every subsequent word in every language is assembled from combinations of primes. If you know the atoms, every molecule is legible. If you don't know the atoms, every molecule is arbitrary.

**What you can do after Phase 1:** You can express any human thought — however primitively — in any target language. You cannot yet speak fluently, but you can never be without language.

---

### Phase 2 — The 12 Schemas (Embodied Spatial Grammars)

**What:** The embodied spatial structures that all languages use to physically ground abstract meaning. These are pre-linguistic — they are how embodied minds structure experience before language applies. Grammar is largely these schemas made obligatory.

**The 12 schemas:**

*Source-Path-Goal* — all directed motion and causation; the structure of narrative, desire, and argument  
*Containment* — inside/outside, inclusion/exclusion, context; the structure of belonging and meaning  
*Force/Compulsion* — push, pull, block, resist, release; the structure of causation and will  
*Contact* — touch, join, separate, link; the structure of relationship and communication  
*Verticality* — up/down, status, life/death, more/less; the structure of value and hierarchy  
*Link/Connection* — bond, send, receive; the structure of communication and relationship  
*Part-Whole* — assembly, division, membership; the structure of identity and analysis  
*Cycle* — rhythm, return, oscillation; the structure of time and natural process  
*Scale/Gradation* — more/less, growth, shrinkage; the structure of comparison  
*Balance* — equilibrium, stability, the righting force; the structure of justice and health  
*Center-Periphery* — core/fringe, essential/marginal; the structure of attention and power  
*Transformation* — becoming, change of state, metamorphosis; the structure of learning and growth  

**How to learn them:** For each schema, learn how each of the five languages encodes it differently. Experience the schema physically first — walk along a path, enter a container, feel a push — before meeting its linguistic encoding. The goal is to feel the schema in your body before you learn the grammar that obligates it.

*Example for Source-Path-Goal in the five languages:*
- Sanskrit: 350 motion roots encoding the quality and moral character of the path
- Japanese: iku/kuru (going-away/coming-toward) plus te-iku/te-kuru directional auxiliaries
- Chinese: modular resultative complements assembling path from direction + base motion
- English: phrasal verbs mapping spatial paths onto abstract states (get through, move on)
- Spanish: ir a + infinitive encoding the future as motion toward a destination

**Why this second:** Because once you know what a grammar rule is physically modeling, you cannot forget it. The subjunctive is not an arbitrary mood — it is the schema for marking the non-real. The ātmanepada/parasmaipada split in Sanskrit is not an arbitrary conjugation — it is the schema for marking who receives the benefit of an action. Schema before rule.

---

### Phase 3 — The 20 Decisions (Mandatory Linguistic Distinctions)

**What:** The distinctions that each language has decided are so important that it made them grammatically obligatory. You cannot speak the language without making these decisions. Understanding them is understanding the language's theory of reality — what it considers too important to leave implicit.

**The mandatory decisions, by language:**

*Sanskrit:*
- Ātmanepada vs. parasmaipada — who benefits from this action? (encoded in every verb)
- Aspect: perfect, imperfect, aorist — what phase of the action are we in?
- Sandhi — the acoustic transformation of words at their boundaries, because sound is not arbitrary

*Japanese:*
- Iru vs. aru — is this being animate or inanimate? (mandatory for any existence claim)
- Transitive vs. intransitive verb pairs — did an agent do this, or did it happen of itself?
- Register and honorifics — what is the relationship between speaker and hearer?
- Speaker-direction in motion — is this going away from or coming toward me?

*Chinese:*
- Aspect particles (了, 着, 过) — is this action completed, ongoing, or experientially encountered?
- Topic prominence — what are we talking about establishes the frame before what happened
- Resultative complements — the result of an action must be specified as part of the verb
- Measure words — every noun class requires a specific counting word

*English:*
- SVO word order — the agent must precede the verb which must precede the patient
- Definite vs. indefinite article — is this a known or unknown, specific or general entity?
- Tense: simple vs. progressive vs. perfect — when and in what phase did this happen?

*Spanish:*
- Ser vs. estar — is this quality essential or contingent?
- Indicative vs. subjunctive — is this real, or is this desired/feared/doubted/hypothetical?
- Preterite vs. imperfect — did this happen as a completed event or as an ongoing background state?

**How to learn them:** Study each language's mandatory distinctions in parallel. For each distinction, ask: what conceptual work is this grammar doing? What does this language consider too important to leave as a contextual inference? The answer reveals the language's philosophy.

---

### Phase 4 — The 8 Roles (Thematic Role Maps)

**What:** Every sentence is a drama staged with roles. The same event can be staged with different distributions of roles — and each staging implies a different theory of causation, agency, and responsibility.

**The 8 roles:**

*Agent* — the volitional doer who intentionally initiates an action  
*Experiencer* — the seat of a mental or emotional state (not acting, but having)  
*Patient/Theme* — the entity undergoing change, moved, affected, or transformed  
*Mover* — the self-propelled subject (moves, but not necessarily volitionally)  
*Recipient/Goal* — the target to whom something is transferred  
*Source* — the origin point from which something departs  
*Instrument/Means* — the tool or method enabling the action  
*Locative/Existential* — the place or context of being  

**How to learn them:** Take a single event — a gift being given, an emotion arising, a movement happening — and express it in all five languages. Notice how each language distributes the roles differently. The philosophical conflict often lives precisely in this redistribution.

*Example with anger:*
- English: "I got angry" (Experiencer receiving incoming force)
- Spanish: "Me enojé" (reflexive Agent — I angered myself, I am responsible)
- Japanese: "腹が立った" (hara ga tatta — the belly stood up: body-part as Mover, no Agent at all)
- Sanskrit: "krodhaḥ samajāyata" (anger arose: Agent absent, anger is an arising Mover)
- Chinese: "我生气了" (wǒ shēngqì le — I produced-anger: Agent actively producing a state)

These are five different theories of emotional causation — and any philosophical debate about "free will in emotions" that ignores these structural differences will be incoherent.

---

### Phase 5 — Root Expansion (Growing Words from Seeds)

**What:** Only now do you learn morphology and derivation — how roots expand into full words, how words inflect for person, number, tense, and mood. But because you already understand the conceptual work being done, every expansion feels necessary rather than arbitrary.

**How to learn it:** Take a single NSM prime and trace its full expansion in each language.

*Example — KNOW:*
- Sanskrit from √jñā: jñāna (knowledge), ajñāna (ignorance = not-knowing), vijñāna (consciousness = knowing-distinctly), prajñā (wisdom = knowing-forth), jñātā (the knower), jñeya (the knowable), jñapti (notification = making-known)
- Japanese from 知る shiru: 知識 chishiki (knowledge), 無知 muchi (ignorance = no-knowing), 未知 michi (unknown = not-yet-known), 知恵 chie (wisdom = knowing-picture)
- Chinese from 知 zhī: 知道 zhīdào (know-the-way), 知识 zhīshi (knowledge), 无知 wúzhī (ignorance), 认知 rènzhī (cognition = recognize-know)

**The goal:** Every new word is seen as a structured combination of already-known atoms. The vocabulary of a language becomes not a list to memorize but a system to read.

---

### The Method as a Whole

| Phase | Content | Duration | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Seeds | 65 NSM primes across all languages | 2-4 weeks | Can express any thought primitively |
| 2 — Schemas | 12 embodied spatial grammars | 4-6 weeks | Grammar rules feel necessary, not arbitrary |
| 3 — Decisions | Each language's mandatory distinctions | 6-8 weeks | Can think in the language's conceptual structure |
| 4 — Roles | Thematic role distribution in each language | 4-6 weeks | Can identify hidden philosophical assumptions in any translation |
| 5 — Expansion | Full morphological and derivational systems | Ongoing | Language becomes transparent as a system |

The method differs from standard language learning in three fundamental ways:

First, it begins with *meaning*, not with sound or script. Sound and script are acquired naturally as you learn the primes and schemas — but they are not the starting point.

Second, it is radically *comparative and simultaneous*. You do not learn one language and then another. You learn all target languages together at the root level, because the differences between them are the most philosophically important information.

Third, it treats grammar as *philosophy made obligatory*. Every grammar rule is an answer to the question: what is so important about reality that speakers of this language must always decide it? When you understand the question, the answer (the grammar rule) becomes obvious.

# Part Three: The Five Primordials
### The Minds Encoded in the Languages

Every language, over millennia of speakers shaping and being shaped by it, produces a characteristic mode of consciousness — a way of being in the world that is not individual personality but collective cognitive architecture. These are the five primordial minds encoded in the five languages of this atlas.

They are not stereotypes of contemporary national cultures. They are the deepest cognitive archetypes that the grammar and root-systems of each language make natural — the minds that the language most easily produces and most powerfully sustains.

---

## The Sanskrit Primordial
### The Process-Seer (Kriyā-Dṛṣṭā · क्रियादृष्टा)

**Epoch:** The Vedic Age — perceiving a world that never stops acting  
**Cosmology:** A universe of interlocking actions, where every object is a frozen verb  
**Core question:** Who benefits from what I am doing?  
**Core anxiety:** Acting without right intention — performing action without dharma  
**Core gift:** The capacity to see moral and ontological texture in every act  

This is a being who wakes into a world of events, not objects. When the Process-Seer looks at a stone, they perceive not a thing but a frozen process — even the Sanskrit word for "sky" (nabhas) traces to a root meaning "to moisten and radiate." Even the ground, bhūmi, is "that which is continuously arising" from √bhū. Their language has no nouns that do not trace to verbs. Every object in their world is secretly an action that has temporarily stabilized.

Their grammar demands a profound moral awareness that exists in no other language with the same systemic force: every verb must declare whether its action serves the doer (ātmanepada — "for oneself") or another (parasmaipada — "for another"). To speak Sanskrit is to be continuously asked, at the grammatical level: *who does this benefit?* This question is not philosophical — it is obligatory. You cannot escape it.

They developed philosophical systems where action is the central category of reality: karma (action as cosmic law — the universe is morally responsive to action), dharma (right action that sustains being — the proper performance of one's role in the cosmic action-web), yoga (disciplined action that transforms the self — action as spiritual technology), and mokṣa (liberation — freedom from the consequences of action by transcending the illusion of being the agent). Their entire spiritual landscape is a meditation on the nature of action.

Their deepest spiritual achievement is the paradox of the Bhagavad Gita: act, but without attachment to the fruits of action. Do everything, but be attached to nothing. The Process-Seer must learn to act fully while dissolving the self that acts. This is the most demanding grammar lesson in the history of consciousness.

**Characteristic philosophies:** Karma, Dharma, Yoga, Vedanta, Mimāṃsā (the philosophy of ritual action), Sāṃkhya  
**Key concepts:** √bhū (being as becoming), ātmanepada/parasmaipada (moral grammar), rasa (aesthetic emotion as cosmic flavor), śruti (heard revelation), avidyā (ignorance as not-seeing)  
**What they gave the world:** The most detailed analysis of action, causation, and moral responsibility ever encoded in a grammar

---

## The Japanese Primordial
### The Texture-Feeler (間の人 — Ma-no-Hito)

**Epoch:** The age of ma — living in the spaces between things  
**Cosmology:** Reality as texture, relationship, and the aching beauty of impermanence  
**Core question:** What is the quality of this moment, this being, this interval?  
**Core anxiety:** Missing the texture of experience by grasping too hard at its content  
**Core gift:** Exquisite sensitivity to relational and sensory gradations invisible to others  

This is a being who lives in a world of texture before substance. Before the Texture-Feeler says anything, they have already perceived: Is this being alive or inert? (iru vs. aru — this is not a choice, it is mandatory). What is their social position relative to mine? (the entire honorific system — keigo — is grammatically unavoidable). What is the quality of this interval, this space between? (ma — 間 — the meaningful pause, the negative space that is itself a kind of presence).

Their language has developed the most elaborate vocabulary of perceptual texture in the world — approximately 3,000 gitaigo (phenomimes and psychomimes) that encode not what things are but how they feel from the inside. Fuwa-fuwa (soft and floating), kira-kira (sparkling with light), doki-doki (heart pounding with anticipation or anxiety), shiku-shiku (quiet persistent weeping), waku-waku (excited anticipation rising from within), biku-biku (flinching with anxiety). These are not metaphors describing feelings. They are primary encodings of pre-verbal felt textures that other languages do not name at all.

They developed the concept of mono no aware (物の哀れ — "the pathos of things") — the deep perception of beauty that arises precisely because things are impermanent. The cherry blossom is beautiful because it falls. Excellence is beautiful because it passes. Excellence that endures becomes mere competence. The Texture-Feeler does not tolerate impermanence — they find it to be the condition of all genuine beauty. This is the aesthetic philosophy generated by a language that makes the organic arising of things (naru) grammatically superior to the forced making of things (suru).

Their deepest social consciousness is organized around uchi/soto (内/外 — inside/outside group), amae (甘え — the comfortable, warm dependence within close relationships that is not weakness but intimacy), and on (恩 — the inescapable debt of gratitude that binds people together). They live in a web of reciprocal obligations that is also a web of love — and these two are not in tension.

**Characteristic philosophies:** Zen Buddhism, Mono no Aware, Wabi-Sabi, Shinto animism, Bushido  
**Key concepts:** Ma (間 — meaningful interval/negative space), Kotodama (言霊 — word-spirit), Naru vs. suru (organic arising vs. forced making), Gitaigo (the vocabulary of sensory texture), Mushin (no-mind — the state where suru becomes naru)  
**What they gave the world:** The understanding that negative space is as real as positive space, and that texture is the primary quality of experience

---

## The Chinese Primordial
### The Compositional Visionary (心眼者 — Xīnyǎn-zhě)

**Epoch:** The age of the Sage-Kings — composing the proper order of heaven and earth  
**Cosmology:** Reality as visual composition — everything is built from visible parts that carry irreducible meaning  
**Core question:** What is the proper composition of this situation?  
**Core anxiety:** Disorder — when names do not match things and social structure dissolves  
**Core gift:** The ability to think in structural compositions and to see meaning encoded in relationships between parts  

This is a being who thinks in visible structures. When the Compositional Visionary encounters any abstract concept, they see its composition immediately. Brightness (明, míng) is sun + moon — clarity is the co-presence of both lights. Rest (休, xiū) is person + tree — rest is a person leaning against wood. Anger (怒, nù) is heart + bondage — rage is the heart in chains. Forgetting (忘, wàng) is heart + death — forgetting is a kind of death of the heart. Every concept is a spatial argument made in visual form.

Their heart (心, xīn) is simultaneously the organ of thought and the organ of feeling — there is no Cartesian split in Chinese consciousness. All mental vocabulary anchors to 心: thinking (思, sī = field + heart: thought grows in the field of the heart), feeling (感, gǎn = heart in motion), forgetting (忘 = heart + death), love (爱, ài = which contains a heart at its center), consciousness itself (意识, yìshi — meaning + knowing). A person who feels badly cannot think clearly, because they are the same organ.

They developed Confucian zhengming (正名 — rectification of names): when names are correct, speech is orderly; when speech is orderly, social affairs can be accomplished; when affairs can be accomplished, ritual is proper; when ritual is proper, punishments fit crimes; when punishments fit crimes, people know what to do. Language is cosmological governance. Wrong words cause civilizational collapse — this is not hyperbole, it is structural analysis.

Their deepest metaphysical concept — Dào (道) — is itself a visual composition: 首 (head) + 辶 (movement) = a head moving forward. The Way is always already a composition of being and movement. To follow the Way is to align your own composition with the composition of the universe. The I Ching (易经 — Book of Changes) is the Compositional Visionary's deepest text: reality is a set of 64 possible compositions of yin and yang lines, and to understand your situation is to identify which composition you are currently in.

**Characteristic philosophies:** Confucianism, Daoism, Chan (Zen) Buddhism, Neo-Confucianism  
**Key concepts:** Zhengming (正名 — rectification of names), Xīn (心 — heart-mind), Lǐ (礼 — ritual propriety as social composition), Dào (道 — the Way), Yuánfèn (缘分 — fated connection as pre-existing thread), Wú wéi (无为 — non-acting as the perfection of action)  
**What they gave the world:** The understanding that meaning is compositional and visual, and that social order is a form of aesthetic composition that can be read and corrected

## The English Primordial
### The Causal Agent (The Doer)

**Epoch:** The age of the agent — who caused this, and how can I change it?  
**Cosmology:** Reality as a field of agents causing effects on patients — the universe is SVO  
**Core question:** Who did this, and what can I do about it?  
**Core anxiety:** Loss of agency — being unable to cause the desired effect  
**Core gift:** The capacity for causal analysis, universalizing abstraction, and empirical action  

This is a being who lives in a universe of causes. The Causal Agent's fundamental question is not "what is this?" (Chinese) or "how does this feel?" (Japanese) or "what is this becoming?" (Sanskrit) but "who did this?" and "what will happen if I do that?" Their language encodes the most rigid agent-action-patient structure of any major language — Subject-Verb-Object, and every deviation from this order is grammatically marked as special.

They carry two vocabularies in permanent uneasy coexistence. The Germanic stratum (blood, breath, bone, feel, know, live, die, give, take, eat, drink, love, hate, think, speak) handles the primal, physical, and intimate. The Latin/French stratum (perceive, cognate, exist, possess, create, destroy, consume, articulate, negotiate) handles the formal, abstract, and distanced. The Causal Agent unconsciously code-switches between bodily intimacy and social formality within a single sentence — a cognitive flexibility that enables both extraordinary social range and a chronic uncertainty about which register is real.

Their deepest cognitive tool is the spatial metaphor systematically mapped onto abstract experience. To "get through" a difficulty. To "break down" under pressure. To "rise above" circumstances. To "fall apart" emotionally. To "move on" from grief. To "come around" to an idea. To "push through" resistance. To "hit a wall." The entire landscape of inner life is a physical landscape traversed by a moving agent who makes choices about direction. The English self is essentially a navigator of its own psyche.

Their language's most philosophically consequential achievement is the collapse of all ontological distinctions into a single verb "be." "She is beautiful" (identity), "she is here" (location), "she is honest" (permanent character), "she is tired" (temporary state) — all "be," with no grammatical distinction. This ontological flatness paradoxically enables radical universalizing abstraction: "All men are created equal" can only be said simply in English because English has a "be" that applies to everything without committing to what kind of being is involved. It is the grammatical foundation of mathematical logic, scientific universalism, and human rights discourse.

**Characteristic philosophies:** Empiricism, Liberalism, Utilitarianism, Analytic Philosophy, Pragmatism  
**Key concepts:** SVO word order (the universe is agent-action-patient), The do/make distinction (process vs. product), Phrasal verbs as embodied spatial metaphors, The ontologically flat "be," The witan/cunnan collapse (knowing-that and knowing-how merged)  
**What they gave the world:** The template for universal claims, the grammar of scientific hypothesis, and the political philosophy of individual agency

## The Spanish Primordial
### The Ontological Discriminator (El Ser-Discernidor)

**Epoch:** The age of the Scholastics — parsing the degrees of being and the grammar of the possible  
**Cosmology:** Reality as a spectrum from pure essence to contingent accident — all being has grades, and all claims have epistemic status  
**Core question:** What kind of being does this have — essential or contingent? Is this real or merely desired/feared/possible?  
**Core anxiety:** Confusing essence with accident — mistaking a temporary condition for a permanent nature  
**Core gift:** Exquisite sensitivity to the ontological status of any claim, and full grammatical command of the entire space of the possible  

This is a being who cannot speak without declaring what kind of being something has. Every Spanish sentence containing "to be" requires a metaphysical commitment: ser (from Latin esse — pure being, essence, definition) marks what something essentially is; estar (from stare — to stand, to be positioned) marks what it is currently doing, how it currently stands, or where it happens to be. "Ella es aburrida" — she is (by nature, essentially) a boring person. "Ella está aburrida" — she is (currently, temporarily) bored. The Spanish Primordial cannot predicate anything of anything without committing to the grade of being involved.

Their subjunctive mood is not a formal or literary register — it is the complete grammatical apparatus for marking the non-real: what is desired (quiero que venga — I want him to come: subjunctive marks the desired-but-not-yet-real), feared (temo que se vaya — I fear she will leave: subjunctive marks the feared-but-not-confirmed), doubted (dudo que sea verdad — I doubt it's true: subjunctive marks the doubted), or hypothetical (si fuera rico — if I were rich: subjunctive marks the contrary-to-fact). The real and the non-real are grammatically distinct realms. The Ontological Discriminator cannot speak without knowing where they stand — in reality or in the space of possibility.

They carry love and will in a single word — querer means both "to want" and "to love." Desire and affection are not separate faculties but the same seeking force operating at different intensities and directed at different objects. What you love most deeply is what you most fundamentally want; what you want most fundamentally is, rightly understood, what you love. The Ontological Discriminator does not separate eros from agape in their grammar — only in their theology.

They have four verbs where English has one for "become" — and each verb encodes a different theory of how change happens. The grammar of becoming in Spanish is a complete philosophy of transformation. They made this distinction obligatory because the question of how change happens — whether voluntarily, involuntarily, gradually, or suddenly — was, to them, the most important question to settle about any event of change.

**Characteristic philosophies:** Scholasticism, Thomism, Existentialism (Unamuno, Ortega y Gasset), Magical Realism as a literary mode, Liberation Theology  
**Key concepts:** Ser vs. estar (essential vs. contingent being), The subjunctive (the grammar of the non-real), Querer (love and desire as one root), The four becoming-verbs (four theories of transformation), Lo real maravilloso (the marvelous real — the belief that reality itself is ontologically strange)  
**What they gave the world:** The grammatical apparatus for distinguishing degrees of being, and the grammar of the entire space of possibility — desire, fear, doubt, and hypothesis as distinct grammatical registers

# Appendix: The Diagnostic Method for Translation Conflicts

When a philosophical conflict arises between languages or traditions, run it through the four layers in sequence:

**Step 1 — NSM Reduction**
Paraphrase both positions using only the 65 NSM primes. Strip out all culturally loaded terms. If the conflict dissolves at this step, it was a false conflict — a conflict of vocabulary, not of genuine philosophical disagreement.

**Step 2 — Verb Class Mapping**
Identify which of Dixon's 20 universal semantic domains the disputed concept belongs to (Motion, Cognition, Emotion, Existence, Possession, etc.). Then ask: what distinctions does each language make mandatory within this domain? The philosophical conflict often lives precisely in these mandatory distinctions — in what one language forces you to decide that another language leaves implicit.

**Step 3 — Image Schema Audit**
Identify the spatial/embodied metaphor that each language uses to ground the disputed abstract concept. Many "philosophical" disagreements are actually disagreements about which spatial metaphor to privilege — understanding as awakening (Sanskrit), as separating (Japanese), as illuminating (Chinese), as supporting from below (English), as grasping together (Spanish). Both can be simultaneously valid as partial perspectives on the same cognitive act.

**Step 4 — Thematic Role Analysis**
Determine whether each language is framing the situation with a different distribution of thematic roles. The anger example — five languages, five different agents and experiencers — is the template. Any debate about free will, moral responsibility, causation, or agency that ignores this level will be arguing about grammatical structure while believing it is arguing about metaphysics.

**The limit of resolution**
Translation conflicts that can be resolved are those where the conflict is about meaning — where Step 1 through 4 reveal a false conflict generated by differing grammatical obligations. Translation conflicts that cannot be fully resolved are those where the conflict is about which mandatory grammatical distinction gets to count as "real" — where one language's grammar is itself the philosophical position. For those, the goal shifts from resolution to mutual intelligibility: making fully explicit what each language is forced to decide, why it is forced to decide it, and what theory of reality that decision encodes.

The deepest translation is always between the primordial minds — between the Process-Seer and the Causal Agent, between the Texture-Feeler and the Ontological Discriminator, between the Compositional Visionary and all of them. This is not a problem to be solved but a territory to be mapped with ever-increasing precision.
