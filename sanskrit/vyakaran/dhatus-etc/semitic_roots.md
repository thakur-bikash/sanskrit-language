# Semitic Language Roots — A Reference Document

> **Scope & Honesty Note:** No single open document lists all ~1,500–2,000 Semitic roots. This file compiles the most important attested and reconstructed roots across **Proto-Semitic, Arabic, and Hebrew**, with cross-references to Aramaic and Akkadian where well-established. For the full scholarly databases, see the **Sources** section at the end. Disputed or uncertain reconstructions are marked with `(??)`.

---

## 1. What is a Semitic Root?

Semitic languages (Arabic, Hebrew, Aramaic, Akkadian, Amharic, Tigrinya, Maltese, etc.) are built on a system of **consonantal roots** — typically **three consonants (triliterals)**, sometimes two (biliterals) or four (quadriliterals). These root consonants carry a core semantic meaning; words are formed by inserting vowel patterns and affixes around the root skeleton.

**Example — root K-T-B (writing/marking):**

| Form | Arabic | Hebrew | Meaning |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Basic verb | *kataba* كَتَبَ | *katav* כָּתַב | he wrote |
| Noun (book) | *kitāb* كِتَاب | *ktav* כְּתָב | writing, script |
| Agent (writer) | *kātib* كَاتِب | *kotev* כּוֹתֵב | writer |
| Place | *maktab* مَكْتَب | *miḥtav* מִכְתָּב | office / letter |
| Passive | *maktūb* مَكْتُوب | *katuv* כָּתוּב | written |

### Root types by consonant count:
- **Biconsonantal (2C):** e.g., *ab* (father), *dam* (blood) — often considered the oldest layer
- **Triconsonantal (3C):** the overwhelming majority, ~95% of vocabulary
- **Quadriconsonantal (4C):** e.g., *tarjama* (to translate), *zalzala* (earthquake)

### Notation conventions used in this document:
- Roots are written with hyphens between consonants: **k-t-b**
- Proto-Semitic reconstructions are prefixed with `*`: **\*k-t-b**
- Arabic script shown where clear
- Uncertain reconstructions flagged: `(??)`
- Language abbreviations: **Ar.** = Arabic, **Heb.** = Hebrew, **Aram.** = Aramaic, **Akk.** = Akkadian, **Eth.** = Ethiopic/Ge'ez, **Ug.** = Ugaritic

## 2. The Semitic Language Family

```
Proto-Semitic (reconstructed, ~3500–3000 BCE)
│
├── East Semitic
│   └── Akkadian (Babylonian, Assyrian) — oldest written Semitic (~2400 BCE)
│       └── Eblaite
│
└── West Semitic
    ├── Northwest Semitic
    │   ├── Canaanite
    │   │   ├── Hebrew (Biblical, Modern)
    │   │   ├── Phoenician / Punic
    │   │   └── Moabite, Ammonite, Edomite
    │   ├── Aramaic (Imperial, Jewish, Syriac, Neo-Aramaic)
    │   └── Ugaritic (~1400 BCE)
    │
    ├── Central Semitic / Arabic
    │   ├── Classical Arabic
    │   ├── Modern Standard Arabic
    │   └── Arabic dialects
    │
    └── South Semitic
        ├── South Arabian (Sabaean, Minaic, Qatabanic)
        └── Ethiopian Semitic
            ├── Ge'ez (Classical Ethiopic)
            ├── Amharic
            ├── Tigrinya
            └── Harari, Gurage, etc.
```

## 4. How to Count "Roots"

The figure of ~1,500–2,000 depends on what you count:

- **Proto-Semitic reconstructed roots:** ~400–600 (those attested in 3+ branches)
- **Classical Arabic triliterals:** ~1,680–1,780 in Quranic Arabic alone; ~2,800+ in Lisan al-Arab
- **Biblical Hebrew roots:** ~1,500–2,000 in the Hebrew Bible (BDB lists ~1,850)
- **Akkadian roots:** ~1,000+ documented in CAD
- **Total across all Semitic:** well over 3,000 if each language counted separately

## 8. Notes on Disputed and Uncertain Areas

1. **Biconsonantal vs. triconsonantal origin:** Many roots like `d-m` (blood), `ʾ-b` (father), `y-d` (hand) are considered ancient biliteral bases. The triliteral system may have developed by adding a third radical later. This is debated.

2. **Arabic emphatics and their Proto-Semitic correspondence:** Arabic has four "emphatic" consonants (ṣ, ḍ, ṭ, ẓ) that do not always correspond neatly to Hebrew emphatics — this creates apparent mismatches in cognate pairs.

3. **Root conflation:** Some Arabic roots that *look* the same (same three letters) may have been originally two different roots that merged. Conversely, what looks like two roots may share a common ancestor.

4. **The N-S-Y / insān problem:** The popular explanation that *insān* (human) derives from *nasiya* (to forget) is a folk etymology debated among scholars. The root *ʾ-n-s* (to be familiar/sociable) is the more likely source.

5. **Quranic roots count:** Careful corpus analysis counts approximately **1,702 distinct roots** in the Quran, covering ~77,430 words.
